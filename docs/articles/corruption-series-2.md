# The Regulators Who Work for the Regulated: A Guide to America's Captured Agencies

*Part 2 of 3: The Systemic Corruption Series*
*Daonra | The Ledger | March 2026*

---

In 2019, Meredith Attwell Baker voted, as an FCC Commissioner, to approve the Comcast-NBC Universal merger. Four months later, she took a job at Comcast.

Four. Months.

That's not a career transition. That's a transaction. And if you think this is a one-off example of someone who just happened to find a great opportunity at the company she just voted to make more powerful, let me introduce you to the entire concept of regulatory capture. Because what happened with Baker isn't the exception. It's the business model.

## What Is Regulatory Capture?

Here's the idea: the government creates agencies to protect the public. The FDA makes sure your drugs won't kill you. The FAA makes sure your plane won't crash. The SEC makes sure Wall Street doesn't steal your retirement. The FCC manages the airwaves. The EPA keeps companies from dumping poison in your water.

In theory.

In practice, these agencies are staffed by people who came from the industries they're supposed to regulate, and who plan to return to those industries after they leave. The regulators don't capture the industry. The industry captures the regulators.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It's documented across every major federal agency. It's the norm.

## The FDA: Brought to You by Pfizer

Let's start with the agency responsible for making sure your medicine is safe.

Nine of the last ten FDA Commissioners went on to work for the pharmaceutical industry or serve on drug company boards after leaving the agency. Nine out of ten. *(Public Citizen)*

The most brazen example: **Dr. Scott Gottlieb**, former FDA Commissioner, joined Pfizer's board of directors less than three months after leaving the agency. Three months. The FDA regulates Pfizer. Gottlieb ran the FDA. Then he went to work for Pfizer. If this were a movie, the audience would say the plot was too on the nose.

It doesn't stop at the top. A 2016 Stanford Law study found that 15 of 26 FDA hematology-oncology reviewers who left the agency went to work for the companies they had been regulating. A separate study published in the BMJ found that 11 of 16 FDA drug reviewers who worked on 28 specific drug approvals took positions at the companies whose drugs they had just approved.

Read that again. The people approving your drugs are doing it knowing their next paycheck might come from the companies making those drugs.

### And Who Funds the FDA?

Here's the part that makes the whole thing even worse: **75% of the FDA's drug review budget** comes from pharmaceutical company user fees, through something called PDUFA (the Prescription Drug User Fee Act). *(Cambridge University Press)*

The FDA's drug review division is funded primarily by the companies whose drugs it reviews. That's like a restaurant health inspector whose salary is paid by the restaurants.

### The Aduhelm Disaster

Want to see what captured regulation looks like in practice? Look at Aduhelm, Biogen's Alzheimer's drug.

Both pivotal clinical trials were stopped early because the drug wasn't working. The FDA's own statistical reviewer recommended against approval. An independent advisory committee voted overwhelmingly against approval. Nearly every independent expert said this drug should not be approved.

The FDA approved it anyway. Price tag: $56,000 per year per patient. *(Cambridge University Press, 2021)*

Three members of the advisory committee resigned in protest. The FDA's own process was overridden. The drug was approved despite the agency's own data and its own advisors saying it shouldn't be. Biogen's stock price jumped 38% in a single day.

## The FAA: Where Boeing Regulates Itself

The Federal Aviation Administration is supposed to certify that aircraft are safe to fly. Instead, it delegated that job to Boeing. Literally. Boeing was certifying its own planes.

Not metaphorically. Not in a "well, they had some input" way. The FAA handed Boeing the authority to approve its own safety assessments. Boeing employees were designated as FAA safety representatives, but they reported to Boeing management, not the FAA. Those employees told investigators they felt **pressured to act in Boeing's interest** and feared retaliation for raising safety concerns. *(POGO, 2019)*

### 346 People Died

The result: two crashes of the Boeing 737 MAX. Lion Air Flight 610 in October 2018. Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302 in March 2019. Three hundred and forty-six people killed.

Investigations revealed the FAA had handed "nearly complete control to Boeing" during the 737 MAX certification process. The agency failed to independently assess MCAS, the automated flight control system that caused both crashes. Boeing knew MCAS had problems. The FAA didn't catch it because Boeing was the one doing the catching.

Here's the kicker: Boeing actually helped draft a bill that weakened government oversight of new airplane designs. That bill became law **weeks before** the first crash. *(POGO, 2019)*

### And Then It Happened Again

You might think that after 346 deaths, Boeing would get its act together. You would be wrong.

In January 2024, a door plug blew out of an Alaska Airlines 737 MAX 9 mid-flight. In the air. At cruising altitude. A section of the fuselage just... left. Passengers were sitting in their seats looking at open sky where a wall used to be.

The investigation found missing bolts. Not damaged bolts. Not incorrectly installed bolts. Missing bolts. The bolts were never put in.

This is the result of a regulatory agency that let the regulated company run the show.

## The SEC: Wall Street's Hall Monitor

The Securities and Exchange Commission exists to protect investors and maintain fair markets. It was created after the 1929 crash because the government recognized that, left unregulated, Wall Street would eat itself and take the economy with it.

How's that going?

Between 2006 and 2010, **219 former SEC employees** sought permission to represent clients before the SEC. Some filed their requests within days of leaving the agency. *(POGO, 2010)*

Former SEC employees routinely help corporations counter investigations, soften enforcement actions, and gain exemptions from federal law. They use their insider knowledge of how the SEC operates to help the companies the SEC is supposed to be investigating. *(Bill Moyers, 2013)*

### The Madoff Failure

Harry Markopolos tried for TEN YEARS to get the SEC to investigate Bernie Madoff's Ponzi scheme. He sent detailed analyses. He provided mathematical proof that the returns were impossible. He literally laid it all out.

The SEC did nothing. For a decade.

Markopolos described the agency as "nonfunctional, captive to the industry." Madoff's scheme eventually collapsed on its own in 2008, having stolen approximately $65 billion from investors. The SEC didn't catch him. He turned himself in.

### The 2008 Financial Crisis: Everyone Got Away With It

The 2008 crash was caused by systemic fraud in the mortgage industry. Fraudulent lending. Fraudulent bundling of mortgages into securities. Fraudulent ratings of those securities. Nine million Americans lost their homes. $11 trillion in household wealth evaporated. 8.7 million jobs disappeared.

The number of bankers who went to prison: one. **Kareem Serageldin**, a mid-level Credit Suisse executive convicted of mismarking bond prices. That's it. One person. For a crisis caused by industry-wide fraud that crashed the global economy. *(Wikipedia; Marketplace)*

For comparison: after the Savings and Loan crisis of the 1980s and 90s, a much smaller scandal, over **1,000 bankers** were convicted and jailed. *(Bill Moyers)*

What changed between the S&L crisis and 2008? The revolving door spun faster. The SEC got weaker. And the financial industry spent billions making sure it stayed that way.

## The FCC: Telecom's Personal Rubber Stamp

The Federal Communications Commission's revolving door is so blatant it's almost impressive.

- **Ajit Pai:** Former Verizon attorney. Became FCC Chairman. Repealed net neutrality, a massive win for internet service providers like his former employer, Verizon, plus Comcast and AT&T.
- **Meredith Attwell Baker:** Voted to approve the Comcast-NBC Universal merger. Took a job at Comcast four months later.
- **Michael Powell:** FCC Chairman. Left to become the chief of NCTA, the cable industry's largest lobbying operation.
- **Dorothy Attwood:** Left the FCC's Wireline Competition Bureau. Became SVP at AT&T, where she helped reshape the rules she had drafted. *(Center for Public Integrity)*

This is the same agency that is supposed to ensure competition, protect consumers, and manage public airwaves in the public interest. Instead, it's a training ground for telecom executives.

## The EPA: Now Featuring the Fossil Fuel Industry

The Environmental Protection Agency was created in 1970 by Richard Nixon. Even Nixon understood that companies couldn't be trusted to regulate their own pollution. That was the bar: too corrupt for Richard Nixon.

As of 2025, that bar has been lowered.

A report found that **43 of 111 nominees and appointees** to agencies like the EPA and the Department of Interior had direct ties to the fossil fuel industry. *(Corporate Knights, 2025)*

All top EPA management positions were filled by former lobbyists and lawyers from the industries the EPA regulates. The agency's leadership included a politician who had received over $400,000 in oil and gas industry backing. *(ToxinFreeUSA, 2025)*

Under this leadership:
- Formaldehyde exposure limits were **doubled**.
- PFAS (forever chemicals) drinking water limits were **rescinded**.
- Toxic pesticide approvals were **fast-tracked**.

The agency tasked with protecting the environment is now run by the people the environment needs protecting from.

## The Pattern

Every agency. Every industry. The same playbook.

1. Work in the industry.
2. Get appointed to the agency that regulates the industry.
3. Weaken regulations while in office.
4. Leave the agency.
5. Return to the industry at a higher salary, cashing in on the favors you did.

It's not corruption in the way most people picture it. Nobody is slipping envelopes of cash under a table. It's worse than that. It's structural. It's legal. It's how the system is designed to operate.

## What Other Countries Do

This doesn't have to be the way things work. Other democracies prove it.

- **France** requires a 3-year cooling-off period before government officials can join the industries they regulated.
- **Canada** imposes a 5-year lobbying ban on senior officials after they leave government.
- The **European Union** maintains a mandatory Transparency Register for lobbyists.

The United States requires a 1 to 2 year cooling-off period, which is widely circumvented through "strategic advisory" roles that technically aren't lobbying.

This is a policy choice. Other countries chose differently. We can too.

## What You Can Do

**1. Support revolving door legislation.** The Revolving Door Project (therevolvingdoorproject.org) tracks industry-government movement and pushes for longer cooling-off periods and stricter conflict-of-interest rules.

**2. Follow agency appointments.** When a new FDA commissioner or FCC chair is nominated, check where they came from. If they spent 20 years at the industry they're about to regulate, that's not "experience." That's a conflict of interest.

**3. Support independent regulatory funding.** The fact that 75% of the FDA's drug review budget comes from pharma companies is fixable. Agencies should be funded by taxpayers, not the industries they oversee.

**4. Push for mandatory recusal rules.** Regulators should be legally required to recuse themselves from decisions involving former (or future) employers. This is basic. Most workplaces have stricter conflict-of-interest policies than the federal government.

**5. Support agencies like POGO and Public Citizen.** The [Project on Government Oversight](https://www.pogo.org/) and [Public Citizen](https://www.citizen.org/) do the investigative work that tracks this revolving door. They need public support because they sure aren't getting industry funding.

The agencies exist. The mandates exist. The expertise exists. What's missing is independence. And independence requires political will, which requires public pressure, which requires you knowing about this in the first place.

Now you know.
