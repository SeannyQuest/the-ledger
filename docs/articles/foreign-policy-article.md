# Your Tax Dollars at Work: The Human Cost of American Foreign Policy

*By Daonra | March 2026*

---

Since September 11, 2001, the United States has spent **$8 trillion** on military operations abroad. The Brown University Costs of War Project estimates the total death toll at **4.5 to 4.7 million people.**

That is not a typo. Four and a half million human beings.

897,000 to 940,000 were killed by direct violence: bombs, bullets, airstrikes. The remaining 3.6 to 3.8 million died from the indirect consequences of war: destroyed hospitals, contaminated water, severed food supply chains, collapsed infrastructure. Children, the elderly, and the sick die first when a society is broken. And we have broken a lot of societies.

This article is long. It needs to be. Because the scope of what has been done in our name, with our money, is staggering. And most Americans have never seen the full picture in one place.

*Sources: Brown University Costs of War Project (costsofwar.watson.brown.edu); Al Jazeera, June 2025*

## Iraq: The Lie That Killed Hundreds of Thousands

On February 5, 2003, Secretary of State Colin Powell sat before the United Nations Security Council and presented "evidence" that Iraq possessed weapons of mass destruction. He held up a vial of fake anthrax. He showed satellite photos of "mobile biological weapons labs." He spoke with the full authority of the United States government.

The primary source was an Iraqi defector codenamed "Curveball" (Rafid Ahmed Alwan al-Janabi), who admitted in 2011 that **he fabricated the entire story.** The mobile labs, the clandestine factories, all of it. He wanted to overthrow Saddam Hussein and told US intelligence what they wanted to hear. US intelligence agencies did not adequately vet his claims. A subsequent investigation concluded the intelligence community was "dead wrong in almost all of its pre-war judgments."

No weapons of mass destruction were ever found. Not one.

The war happened anyway.

### The Dead

The Iraq Body Count project documented **186,901 to 210,296 violent civilian deaths** through 2020. A PLOS Medicine study estimated approximately 461,000 "excess deaths" from 2003 to 2011 alone, with over 60% caused directly by violence.

The Brown Costs of War Project estimates 134,000 or more civilians were among the roughly 190,000 people killed by direct violence in Iraq. These are not combatants. These are people who were alive before we arrived and dead after.

### The Destabilization

The US disbanded Iraq's entire military. It purged the government through "de-Baathification." It created a power vacuum that swallowed the country whole.

The consequences: a sectarian civil war from 2006 to 2008. The rise of al-Qaeda in Iraq, which became ISIS. ISIS conquered vast territory across Iraq and Syria by 2014. A second US military intervention was then required to defeat the force that the first intervention created.

We broke Iraq. Then we spent more money and more lives trying to fix what we broke. And it is still broken.

### The Cost

The government told Americans the Iraq war would cost $50 to $60 billion. It cost approximately **$2 trillion** in direct costs. That is roughly $8,000 per American taxpayer. With interest on war borrowing projected through 2053, the total reaches $3.9 trillion. Economist Joseph Stiglitz estimated the full long-term cost at over $3 trillion.

The government's estimate was off by a factor of 40.

### Who Profited

**Halliburton/KBR** received $39.5 billion in federal contracts. Halliburton's Iraq revenue: $20.1 billion. Its former CEO was Vice President Dick Cheney, one of the war's chief architects. A Pentagon audit found KBR could not account for $1.8 billion worth of work.

**Blackwater** (now Academi), founded by Erik Prince, earned over $1 billion from Iraq contracts, primarily through no-bid deals. In 2007, Blackwater contractors killed 17 Iraqi civilians in Nisour Square, Baghdad. Four contractors were convicted. All four were pardoned by President Trump in 2020.

The people who pushed for the war got richer. The people who fought it got PTSD. The people who lived through it got killed.

*Sources: The Guardian (Curveball admission, 2011); Commission on WMD Report; Iraq Body Count; PLOS Medicine; Brown Costs of War; Business Insider; ICIJ (Halliburton contracts); Brookings (Blackwater)*

## Yemen: The War You're Not Supposed to See

Yemen has been called the worst humanitarian crisis on Earth. Here is why.

In 2015, Saudi Arabia formed a coalition to intervene in Yemen's civil war. The United States backed it with weapons sales, logistical support, intelligence sharing, in-flight refueling for Saudi jets (until 2018), and US contractors servicing Saudi aircraft. A "substantial portion" of airstrikes that killed civilians were carried out by jets developed, sold, and maintained by US companies, flown by US-trained pilots (Columbia Magazine).

By the end of 2021: **377,000 people dead.** Over 150,000 killed directly by armed conflict. Approximately 15,000 civilians killed by direct military action, mostly from Saudi coalition airstrikes.

In 2024, 18.2 million Yemenis, 55% of the entire population, needed humanitarian assistance. 17.6 million were experiencing severe food insecurity. 610,000 children were projected to be acutely malnourished. 62% of households reported inadequate food consumption in July 2024, the highest ever recorded.

The World Food Programme could only provide rations equaling **17% of the recommended daily intake.** Because of funding shortfalls. In the richest era of human history.

Yemen experienced one of the largest cholera outbreaks in recorded history: over 2.5 million suspected cases since 2016. Coalition airstrikes destroyed the water and sanitation infrastructure that could have prevented it.

### Direct US Strikes

The US did not just arm the Saudis. It struck Yemen directly.

**Operation Poseidon Archer** (January 2024 to January 2025): Strikes against Houthis killed 21 civilians.

**Operation Rough Rider** (March to May 2025): 339 strikes. 238 civilians killed, including 24 children. 467 injured.

**Ras Isa Port strike** (April 2025): A single strike on a fuel storage facility killed 84 civilians.

These numbers represent individual human beings. Families. Children who had nothing to do with geopolitics.

### The Blockade

The Saudi-led coalition enforced a naval and air blockade restricting food, fuel, and medicine. This blockade was the primary driver of the famine. When the UN Group of Eminent Experts tried to investigate, Saudi Arabia and the UAE successfully lobbied to block the mandate in 2021, eliminating independent monitoring.

We armed the coalition. We fueled their jets. We shared targeting intelligence. We watched the blockade starve millions of people. And when someone tried to investigate, our allies shut it down.

*Sources: IOM Crisis Response; ReliefWeb; Concern Worldwide; Genocide Watch; HRW Yemen Report 2024; Columbia Magazine; ECDHR*

## Palestine: $317.9 Billion and Counting

The United States has provided Israel with more foreign aid than any other country in history. Since 1951, adjusted for inflation, the total is **$317.9 billion** (Brown Costs of War).

The current agreement, signed in 2019, provides $3.8 billion per year: $3.3 billion in military financing plus $500 million for missile defense. Under its terms, Israel must spend 100% of the military financing in the United States. The money flows from American taxpayers to the Israeli military to American defense contractors. It is a subsidy for weapons manufacturers dressed up as foreign aid.

Since October 7, 2023, the US has provided an additional **$21.7 billion** in military aid. In the first year of the Gaza war alone: $17.9 billion, the most military aid sent to Israel in a single year, adjusted for inflation (AP News).

### Gaza: The Numbers

After the Hamas attack on October 7, 2023, Israel launched a military campaign in Gaza. Here is the documented death toll:

The Gaza Ministry of Health reported 67,075 killed and 169,430 injured as of October 2025. A study published in *The Lancet Global Health* (February 2026) estimated over 75,000 killed in the first 16 months. A separate peer-reviewed *Lancet* analysis estimated 64,260 deaths from traumatic injury through June 2024. According to demographic analysis, 56% of documented deaths were women, children, and elderly.

The lethality averaged 312 Gazan deaths per day from October through December 2023.

We will not make light of these numbers. They represent an almost incomprehensible scale of human suffering.

### The International Court of Justice

On January 26, 2024, the ICJ issued provisional measures in South Africa's genocide case against Israel, ordering that Israel must refrain from acts under the Genocide Convention, prevent and punish incitement to genocide, ensure provision of humanitarian assistance, and preserve evidence related to the allegations.

On May 24, 2024, the ICJ ordered Israel to halt its military offensive in Rafah.

Human Rights Watch reported in February 2024 that Israel had failed to comply with at least one measure: the provision of humanitarian aid.

### The Veto Shield

The United States has vetoed over 45 resolutions critical of Israel in the UN Security Council, more than for any other country. During the Gaza war alone, the US vetoed resolutions calling for humanitarian pauses (October 2023), a ceasefire (December 2023), an immediate and permanent ceasefire backed by all 10 elected council members (June 2025), and an unconditional ceasefire and hostage release (September 2025).

Each time, the US stood alone or nearly alone against the rest of the international community.

## The Drone Program: Killing by Algorithm

The United States operates a global assassination program using unmanned aerial vehicles. There are two types of strikes.

**Personality strikes** target a specific, named individual. **Signature strikes** target groups of people based on their behavior patterns, without knowing who they are.

Read that second one again. The US government kills people without knowing their names, based on what their movements look like from 10,000 feet up.

The Obama administration developed the "Disposition Matrix," a database for tracking, capturing, or killing suspected enemies. The president personally approved targets at weekly counterterrorism meetings. The legal justification: the Justice Department asserted the president could authorize killing US citizens if they posed an "imminent threat," using a definition of "imminent" that did not require evidence of a specific plot.

### Military-Age Males: Guilty Until Proven Dead

For "signature strikes," the US government classified military-aged males in strike zones as combatants unless proven otherwise. Posthumously.

If you were a young man in rural Pakistan, Yemen, or Somalia, and a drone killed you, you were counted as an enemy combatant by default. Your family had to prove your innocence after you were already dead.

The Bureau of Investigative Journalism estimated 800 to 1,750 civilians were killed by drone strikes from 2002 to 2020, out of 10,000 to 17,000 total killed. Airwars, which tracks the broader War on Terror, estimated 22,679 to 48,308 civilian deaths.

### The Double Tap

The US employed a tactic called the "double tap." Strike a target. Wait for first responders and rescuers to arrive. Then strike again.

A bomb hits a house. Neighbors, family members, and medics rush to help the wounded. A second bomb hits them.

When other armed groups use this tactic, the United States calls it terrorism.

### Abdulrahman al-Awlaki: 16 Years Old, American Citizen

On October 14, 2011, a US drone strike in Yemen killed Abdulrahman al-Awlaki. He was 16 years old. He was an American citizen, born in Denver, Colorado. He was eating dinner with his cousin at an outdoor restaurant.

Two weeks earlier, a separate strike had killed his father, Anwar al-Awlaki, an American citizen accused of being an al-Qaeda leader. The father's killing was controversial but at least targeted.

Abdulrahman was not the target. US officials called his death a mistake. In January 2017, a US raid in Yemen killed his 8-year-old sister, Nawar al-Awlaki. Also an American citizen.

No one was ever held accountable for any of these deaths.

## Covert Operations: A History of Overthrowing Democracies

The CIA has a documented pattern of overthrowing democratically elected governments when those governments threaten American corporate interests. The pattern is consistent: a democratic government nationalizes resources. An American corporation loses profits. The government is labeled "communist." The CIA orchestrates a coup. A military dictatorship takes power. American corporate access is restored. The population suffers for decades.

**Iran, 1953.** Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh nationalized Iran's oil industry, previously controlled by the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (now BP). The CIA and British intelligence fomented unrest, bribed officials, organized riots. The Shah was restored to power. Twenty-six years of authoritarian rule followed, leading to the 1979 Islamic Revolution and decades of US-Iran hostility that continue today. In 2023, the CIA formally acknowledged the coup and described it as "undemocratic."

**Guatemala, 1954.** President Jacobo Árbenz enacted land reform that included nationalizing unused land owned by the United Fruit Company (now Chiquita). CIA Director Allen Dulles was a former president of United Fruit's board. His brother, Secretary of State John Foster Dulles, was former legal counsel to United Fruit. The CIA financed and trained rebels, launched psychological warfare, used CIA-piloted planes. Árbenz was overthrown. What followed: 40 years of US-backed authoritarian regimes, civil war, widespread torture, and genocide against indigenous Maya populations. 200,000 people were killed.

**Chile, 1973.** President Salvador Allende, democratically elected, was overthrown in a military coup supported by the CIA. General Augusto Pinochet seized power. Seventeen years of military dictatorship followed. Thousands were tortured, killed, or "disappeared."

**Operation Condor (1975-1983).** A coordinated campaign of political repression by right-wing military dictatorships in Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Chile, Paraguay, and Uruguay, all backed and financed by the United States. Cross-border abductions. Torture. Assassinations. Tens of thousands killed or disappeared across the continent.

The full list extends further: Congo (1960), Brazil (1964), Indonesia (1965-66, where 500,000 to 1,000,000 were killed), Greece (1967), Cambodia (1970), Nicaragua (1980s), Panama (1989), Haiti (1991 and 2004), Honduras (2009), Libya (2011).

This is not ancient history. This is the country we live in.

*Sources: PBS; Britannica; National Security Archive; The Guardian; History News Network*

## The Money Machine

In 2024, the United States spent **$997 billion** on its military. Nearly one trillion dollars. That is 37% of all global military spending. More than the next nine countries combined: China ($314B), Russia ($149B), Germany ($88.5B), India ($86.1B), the UK, France, Japan, South Korea, and Australia.

That is like showing up to a neighborhood basketball game in a fighter jet.

### Where the Money Goes

The top four defense contractors in 2023: Lockheed Martin ($67.6B revenue), RTX/Raytheon ($68.9B), Boeing ($77.8B), and Northrop Grumman ($39.3B). Their CEOs earned between $18 million and $32.8 million each. Boeing's CEO got a 45% raise the same year the company posted a $2.2 billion loss. In the defense sector, failure is someone else's problem. Usually the taxpayer's.

### The Closed Loop

Since 2001, weapons manufacturers have spent over **$2.5 billion** on lobbying. In 2025 alone: $293.3 million, a record, up 25% from the previous record. They target members of the Armed Services and Appropriations committees specifically.

Between 2014 and 2019, over 1,700 senior military and civilian officials transitioned to defense contractor roles. In 2018, the top 20 defense contractors hired 645 former senior government officials. Nearly 90% became registered lobbyists (POGO).

The same people who decide which weapons to buy become the people selling those weapons. Then they lobby their former colleagues to buy more. Defense industry campaign donations exceeded $50 million in the 2020 cycle.

Taxpayer dollars go to Congress, which authorizes spending. The Pentagon awards contracts. Defense contractors take profits and pay executives tens of millions. The contractors spend hundreds of millions on lobbying and campaign donations. Congress votes for more military spending. Repeat.

## What That Money Could Buy Instead

With $997 billion, the United States could fund:

**Universal healthcare.** A single-payer system would save an estimated $450 billion per year compared to current spending, while covering every American (Yale/Lancet study). We do not need *more* money for healthcare. We need to stop wasting what we already spend.

**Free public college for everyone.** Cost: $28 to $75 billion per year, depending on the model. You could make college tuition-free and still have over $900 billion left.

**Eliminate all student loan debt.** Total outstanding: $1.84 trillion. The entire amount could be erased with less than two years of the defense budget.

**Close the infrastructure gap.** The American Society of Civil Engineers estimates a $3.7 trillion funding gap in US infrastructure. The defense budget for 3.7 years would close it entirely.

And here is a detail that rarely gets mentioned: $1 billion spent on healthcare creates nearly twice as many jobs as $1 billion spent on defense (NIH study).

*Sources: Georgetown; ASCE; NIH; Yale/Lancet*

## The Veterans: Sent to Fight, Left to Die

The people who actually fight these wars are overwhelmingly working-class Americans who joined for economic opportunity, college tuition, or a way out. When they come home, the system that sent them abandons them.

**Suicide:** Approximately 20 veterans die by suicide every day. The veteran suicide rate is 33.9 per 100,000, which is 57.3% higher than non-veteran adults. Suicide is the second-leading cause of death for veterans under 45. (VA.gov)

**PTSD:** 23% of veterans using VA healthcare have been diagnosed with PTSD at some point. Women veterans are disproportionately affected, partly due to military sexual trauma. (VA)

**Homelessness:** 32,882 veterans were experiencing homelessness in 2024. Nearly 14,000 were unsheltered. (VA)

**VA failures:** A 2023 Inspector General report found the Veterans Crisis Line provided "subpar help" to a Texas veteran who later died by suicide. A GAO report found VA suicide prevention teams experienced burnout and understaffing. ProPublica documented systematic failures including botched screenings and lost patients.

We spend $997 billion a year on the military. We cannot keep the people who served in it alive.

*Sources: VA.gov; Military Times; Time; ProPublica; GovExec*

## Sanctions: The Quiet Killer

US economic sanctions are presented as a peaceful alternative to war. In practice, they function as collective punishment of civilian populations.

**Iraq in the 1990s:** UNICEF estimated 400,000 to 500,000 excess deaths among children under five between 1984 and 1999 during the sanctions period. In a 1996 interview, journalist Lesley Stahl asked Ambassador Madeleine Albright: "We have heard that half a million children have died. That is more children than died in Hiroshima. Is the price worth it?" Albright responded: "I think that is a very hard choice, but the price, we think, the price is worth it."

Half a million children. Worth it.

**Iran:** International banks over-comply with sanctions, refusing to process legitimate medical transactions. Shortages of critical drugs for epilepsy, cancer, hemophilia, and HIV. During COVID-19, sanctions blocked vaccine shipments. (HRW)

**Cuba:** The embargo has been in place since 1962. Over 60 years. Critical shortages of food, fuel, and medications. The UN General Assembly has voted to condemn the embargo 31 consecutive times. The US and usually only Israel vote against.

**Venezuela:** An estimated 40,000 excess deaths between 2017 and 2018 during major sanctions escalation. Over 300,000 Venezuelans lost access to healthcare, including HIV patients, dialysis patients, and cancer patients. (CEPR)

**The global toll:** A study in *The Lancet Global Health* estimated that unilateral sanctions are associated with **564,258 deaths annually** worldwide between 2010 and 2021. Children under five and the elderly are most affected.

Sanctions kill as many people as wars. They just do it more quietly.

*Sources: Global Policy Forum; Democracy Now; HRW; Oxfam; OHCHR; CEPR; Common Dreams*

## What You Don't See (By Design)

In 1988, Edward Herman and Noam Chomsky published *Manufacturing Consent*, identifying how corporate media ownership, advertising dependence, government sourcing, pressure campaigns against dissent, and ideological framing combine to produce news coverage that makes wars seem necessary and alternatives seem impossible.

During the 2003 Iraq invasion, the Pentagon placed reporters inside military units. "Embedded journalism" meant coverage from the perspective of the barrel of a gun, not from the bombed neighborhood. Independent journalists who tried to report from the civilian perspective were sometimes killed. Al Jazeera's Baghdad bureau was struck by US missiles in 2003, killing correspondent Tareq Ayyoub.

The Pentagon maintains an Entertainment Media Office that offers Hollywood access to military equipment in exchange for script approval. Films that portray the military favorably get F-16s and aircraft carriers for free. Films that portray it critically get nothing. *Top Gun* (1986) boosted Navy recruitment by 500%. *Top Gun: Maverick* (2022) was a $1.5 billion recruitment ad with full Pentagon cooperation. Meanwhile, *Platoon*, *Apocalypse Now*, and *Full Metal Jacket*, among the greatest war films ever made, received no military support because they depicted war honestly.

The Pentagon has influenced thousands of films and TV shows. It is the largest, most sustained propaganda operation in American history. Funded by taxpayers.

When a US drone kills a family in Yemen, it might get a brief mention on page 12. When a foreign attack kills Americans, it dominates the news cycle for weeks. Arms sales are announced in press releases that read like product launches. The connection between "US approves $2 billion arms deal with Saudi Arabia" and "Saudi airstrike kills 40 children on a school bus in Yemen" is never drawn on the same broadcast.
