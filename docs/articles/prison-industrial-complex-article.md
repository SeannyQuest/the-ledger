# America Spends $4.4 Billion a Year Locking People Up for Profit. Here's Who's Cashing the Checks.

*The prison-industrial complex isn't broken. It's working exactly as designed.*

---

In 2003, a judge in Luzerne County, Pennsylvania named Mark Ciavarella Jr. started sending children to a for-profit detention center for things like mocking a teacher on MySpace. Jaywalking. Driving over a curb. Kids as young as 13 were shackled and hauled away without lawyers, without due process, sometimes without being allowed to say goodbye to their parents.

He did this for five years. Four thousand convictions. Twenty-three hundred children.

His price? $2.8 million in kickbacks from the company that built the detention centers. (CBS News, 2022)

Some of those kids later died by suicide or overdose. Ciavarella got 28 years.

That's the version of this story where someone actually went to prison. The rest of the prison-industrial complex operates on the same logic, just legally. And the people running it are making a hell of a lot more than $2.8 million.

---

## The $4.4 Billion Industry You're Funding

Two companies dominate the private prison business in America: **CoreCivic** (formerly the Corrections Corporation of America, because even they knew that name was a bit on-the-nose) and **The GEO Group**.

In 2024, CoreCivic pulled in **$2.0 billion in revenue**. GEO Group brought in **$2.42 billion**. Combined, that's **$4.42 billion**. In a single year. From locking human beings in cages. (CoreCivic and GEO Group 2024 Annual Reports)

GEO Group's stock returned **158.36%** in 2024. If you'd invested in imprisoning people at the start of that year, you would have more than doubled your money. Let that sit with you for a second.

And what did CoreCivic do with its profits? It spent **$68.5 million buying back its own stock**. Not improving conditions. Not reducing recidivism. Buying. Back. Stock. (CoreCivic 2024 Annual Report)

## How Much Does It Cost to Buy a Law?

Between the two companies, CoreCivic and GEO Group spent **$5.8 million on lobbying in 2024 alone**. Since 2000, private prison companies have funneled **over $6 million to state politicians** and **$835,000 to federal politicians** through PACs. (OpenSecrets; NOTUS)

And it's not just direct lobbying. Enter **ALEC**, the American Legislative Exchange Council, a cozy little nonprofit where corporate lobbyists and state legislators sit in a room together and literally co-write legislation. During the 1990s, when "tough on crime" laws were spreading across the country like a particularly profitable virus, the Corrections Corporation of America served as **private sector co-chair** of ALEC's Public Safety Taskforce. (ALEC Exposed; The Nation)

The model bills ALEC drafted include gems like the "Private Correctional Facilities Act" (enables government contracting with for-profit prisons), the "Prison Industries Act" (permits companies to use ultra-cheap prison labor), and the legislation that became Arizona's SB 1070, the controversial immigration law that just happened to massively increase immigration detentions. Which just happened to be a major revenue stream for private prisons. What a coincidence. (Justice Policy Institute; Prison Legal News)

Here's the business model distilled to its essence: private prisons have contractual **minimum occupancy guarantees of 80 to 90 percent**. That means the government either fills the beds or pays for the empty ones. The financial incentive isn't rehabilitation. It isn't public safety. It's *bodies*. (American Bar Association)

## 500% and Counting

Let's rewind. In 1970, about **196,000 people** were in American prisons. Today, that number is roughly **1.85 million** across federal prisons, state prisons, and local jails. That's a **500% increase**. (Bureau of Justice Statistics; Vera Institute of Justice)

The U.S. incarcerates people at a rate of **531 per 100,000 residents**. Germany does 69 per 100,000. Norway does 54. Even Massachusetts, the state with America's lowest incarceration rate, would rank among the highest nations globally. (World Prison Brief; Prison Policy Initiative)

To be clear: this explosion didn't happen because Americans suddenly became 500% more criminal. Crime rates don't come close to explaining it. Policy does.

### The War on Drugs: The Quiet Part Out Loud

In 1971, Richard Nixon declared drug abuse "public enemy number one." Years later, his domestic policy chief **John Ehrlichman** said the quiet part out loud in an interview with journalist Dan Baum:

> "The Nixon campaign in 1968, and the Nixon White House after that, had two enemies: the antiwar left and black people. We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities." (Harper's Magazine, 2016)

That's not speculation. That's not a conspiracy theory. That's the guy who designed the policy telling you what the policy was for.

Reagan doubled down in the 1980s with the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986, which created **mandatory minimum sentences** and the infamous **100:1 crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity**. Five grams of crack (predominantly used in Black communities) triggered the same five-year mandatory minimum as 500 grams of powder cocaine (predominantly used by white people). Same drug. Same chemical compound. One hundred times the punishment. (Drug Policy Alliance; Brennan Center)

Then Clinton signed the **Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act of 1994**, which mandated life sentences through three-strikes provisions. Under California's version of the law, the third "strike" could be **any felony**. People got 25-to-life for stealing a slice of pizza. This is real. This actually happened. In America. (Brennan Center; ACLU)

The result? People incarcerated for nonviolent drug offenses went from **50,000 in 1980 to 400,000 in 1997**. An 800% increase in 17 years, for crimes that didn't involve a single victim. (Vera Institute of Justice)

## The Racism Is the Feature, Not the Bug

Drug usage rates are **roughly equal** across racial groups. Read that again. Roughly. Equal.

Now look at the outcomes:

- Black men are **6 times more likely** to be arrested for drug offenses than white men. (Human Rights Watch)
- Black men are sent to state prison on drug charges at **13 times the rate** of white men. (Prison Policy Initiative)
- Black males receive sentences **20% longer** than white males for similar federal crimes. (United States Sentencing Commission)
- Federal prosecutors are **65% more likely** to file charges carrying mandatory minimums against Black defendants. (NACDL)

The Black incarceration rate went from roughly **600 per 100,000 in 1970 to 1,808 per 100,000 in 2000**. That's not a war on drugs. That's a war on Black people, using drugs as the legal justification. John Ehrlichman told you so. (Bureau of Justice Statistics)

And it starts young. Black students make up **15.1%** of school enrollment but account for **31.6% of school arrests**. Black girls are **6 times more likely** to be suspended than white girls. Students suspended once are **twice as likely to drop out**. Students arrested at school are **three times as likely to drop out**. It's a pipeline. That's not a metaphor. It's a documented, measurable, deliberate pipeline from school desks to prison bunks. (ACLU; APA, 2017-2018 data)

## Slavery with a Rebrand

Here is the text of the 13th Amendment to the United States Constitution:

> "Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, **except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted**, shall exist within the United States."

Read that exception one more time. The amendment that "abolished" slavery explicitly carved out an exception for prisoners. And America has been exploiting it for 160 years.

Average prison wages range from **$0.14 to $2.00 per hour**. The national average is about **$0.63**. **Eight states pay nothing at all** for most prison labor: Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Mississippi, Oklahoma, South Carolina, and Texas. In Louisiana, some prison work pays **two cents an hour**. (Prison Policy Initiative; ACLU)

Two cents. Not two dollars. Two pennies.

Refusal to work can result in **solitary confinement** or loss of privileges. So it's not voluntary. And the people profiting include some of the biggest names in American business: **McDonald's, Walmart, Starbucks, Whole Foods, Victoria's Secret, Verizon, American Airlines, IBM**. They've all used prison labor, directly or through supply chains. Companies even receive a **$2,400 tax credit per work-release inmate** through the Work Opportunity Tax Credit. (UC Berkeley; Economic Policy Institute; Walk Free Foundation)

So let's be precise about what's happening: incarcerated people, disproportionately Black, working for pennies or nothing, under threat of punishment, producing goods for Fortune 500 companies that receive a tax break for using them. If you need a minute, take one.

## Half a Million People in Jail Who Haven't Been Convicted of Anything

Over **70 to 80 percent** of people in local jails haven't been convicted. They're waiting for trial. Nearly **half a million human beings** are locked up not because they've been found guilty, but because they can't afford bail. (ACLU; Vera Institute)

**Kalief Browder** was 16 when he was arrested in the Bronx for allegedly stealing a backpack. His bail was set at $3,000. His family couldn't pay. He was sent to Rikers Island, where he spent **three years** waiting for a trial that never happened. Nearly **two years in solitary confinement**. He was beaten by inmates and guards. He attempted suicide multiple times. He refused every plea deal because he hadn't done anything wrong. (The New Yorker, 2014)

In 2013, the charges were dismissed. The prosecutors couldn't even find the alleged victim.

After his release, Kalief told *The New Yorker*: "I'm not all right. I'm messed up."

In June 2015, Kalief Browder died by suicide. He was 22 years old.

The system that destroyed him is still running. Pretrial detention has **increased 268% since 1985**. (The Marshall Project; Stanford University)

## What Happens After: The Permanent Punishment

Getting out doesn't mean getting free.

Roughly **4 million Americans** can't vote because of felony disenfranchisement. **One in 22 Black Americans** of voting age is stripped of that right, three times the rate of non-Black Americans. In Florida and Tennessee, **over 6%** of the entire adult population cannot vote. (The Sentencing Project, 2024)

Formerly incarcerated people face an unemployment rate **five times** the general population. A criminal record cuts employer callbacks in half. Many professions are completely off-limits: cosmetology, real estate, teaching, healthcare. Nearly **50,000 legal restrictions** exist that prevent reintegration. (American Progress; Vera Institute)

They're also **10 times more likely** to experience homelessness. Private landlords routinely refuse tenants with records. Public housing can ban them for **3 to 10 years**. (The Marshall Project)

No job. No home. No vote. No stability. Back to prison. Roughly **70% of released prisoners** return within five years. (Bureau of Justice Statistics)

Compare that to Norway, where the recidivism rate is **20%**. Norway treats imprisonment as the loss of liberty, full stop, not as a license for further degradation. Small facilities. Education. Vocational training. Staff trained as mentors. Family connections maintained. It's not a fantasy. It's a policy choice. (Wikipedia; Justice Trends)

America's system isn't failing to rehabilitate people. It isn't trying to.

## The Human Toll in Numbers You Can't Ignore

- **122,000 people** in solitary confinement on any given day. The UN defines more than 15 consecutive days as torture. The average stay in the U.S. is 1 to 3 months. **5.7% spend over six years.** (AFSC; Vera Institute, 2023)
- **66% of federal prisoners** report receiving no mental health care while incarcerated. (Bureau of Justice Statistics)
- **4,895 sexual assaults** substantiated between 2016 and 2018, averaging more than four per day. Those are the *reported* ones. (Bureau of Justice Statistics)
- Each year in prison reduces life expectancy by an estimated **two years**. (Council on Criminal Justice)
- **2.7 million children** in the U.S. have a parent in prison. (Urban Institute)

## People Who Are Actually Fighting This

This isn't a "thoughts and prayers" situation. Organizations with real track records exist:

**Bryan Stevenson and the Equal Justice Initiative** secured a Supreme Court ruling banning mandatory life-without-parole for children. They've freed or provided relief to **over 140 wrongly condemned death row prisoners**. Stevenson's book *Just Mercy* brought these stories to millions. (EJI)

**The Innocence Project** has exonerated **375+ wrongfully convicted people** through DNA evidence. Three hundred and seventy-five. Each one a person who was in prison for something they did not do. (The Innocence Project)

**The ACLU's Smart Justice campaign** is working to cut the U.S. prison population in half through sentencing reform, bail reform, and challenging the ~50,000 legal restrictions on formerly incarcerated people. (ACLU)

**The Bail Project** pays bail for low-income individuals to prevent the exact thing that happened to Kalief Browder. **The Marshall Project** does investigative journalism that keeps this in the public eye. **The Sentencing Project** produces the research that makes these arguments impossible to dismiss. (The Bail Project; The Marshall Project; The Sentencing Project)

## What You Can Do Right Now

This is the part where we refuse to leave you with just rage.

1. **Know your local prosecutors.** District attorneys have more power over incarceration than almost any other elected official. They decide who gets charged, what gets dropped, and whether to pursue mandatory minimums. Most of them run unopposed. Find out who yours is. Find out how they use that power. Vote accordingly.

2. **Support the organizations doing the work.** EJI, The Bail Project, The Innocence Project, The Sentencing Project, and the ACLU's Smart Justice campaign are all taking direct, measurable action. Dollars and volunteers go further than tweets.

3. **Push for bail reform in your state.** New Jersey, New York, and Illinois have all implemented versions of bail reform. If your state hasn't, ask your representatives why. Repeatedly.

4. **Look at what you're buying.** Companies that use prison labor don't advertise it. The ACLU and Worth Rises maintain lists of corporations profiting from incarceration. Your purchasing power is a tool. Use it.

5. **Talk about this.** Not in a "did you know" way. In a "two companies made $4.4 billion last year from caging human beings and their stock went up 158%" way. The specific numbers are what make people pay attention. Use them.

6. **Support formerly incarcerated people directly.** Ban-the-box hiring initiatives, second-chance housing programs, and re-entry organizations in your community need advocates. The 50,000 legal barriers to reintegration don't dismantle themselves.

The United States locks up more people than any other democracy on Earth. It costs billions. It doesn't reduce crime. It targets Black and brown communities with mathematical precision. It pays prisoners pennies to work for Fortune 500 companies. It destroys families. It kills people. And two publicly traded corporations and their shareholders profit from every single day of it.

This is not a broken system. A broken system implies something went wrong. Nothing went wrong. The people who designed it got exactly what they wanted. The question is whether the rest of us are going to keep letting them have it.
