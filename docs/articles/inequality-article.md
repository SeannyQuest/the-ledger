# Three People Own More Than 165 Million Americans Combined: A Field Guide to Inequality

*By Daonra | March 2026*

---

Elon Musk is worth $428 billion. The median American household is worth $193,000. If the median household's wealth were a single dollar bill, Musk would have **$2.2 million.**

That is not a metaphor. That is division.

The top 1% of Americans own 31% of all wealth in the country, roughly $49 trillion. The bottom 50%, over 165 million people, own 2.4%. The top three richest Americans (Musk, Ellison, Zuckerberg) hold more wealth combined than that entire bottom half.

And it is getting worse. Here is the full picture, number by number, with every figure sourced and every comparison designed to make the abstract concrete.

*Sources: Federal Reserve Distributional Financial Accounts; Forbes 400 (2025)*

---

## CEO Pay: A Comparison in Lunches

In 1965, the average CEO of a major American company earned 21 times what their typical worker made. By 2024, that ratio was **281 to 1.**

The average CEO of a top-350 firm earned $23 million in 2024. Seventy-nine percent of that came from stock-based compensation. Since 1978, CEO pay has increased **1,094%.** Worker pay over the same period? Up 26%.

Let's make that real. A CEO at a top-350 firm makes more **by lunch on January 2nd** than their average worker earns in the entire year.

That is not a market outcome. That is a system designed to funnel money upward. Since 1978, CEO compensation has grown 46 times faster than the pay of the people who actually do the work.

*Sources: Economic Policy Institute (2024 CEO compensation data)*

## The Minimum Wage: A 68-Year Low

The federal minimum wage is $7.25 an hour. It has been $7.25 an hour since July 2009. That is the longest period without an increase in American history.

At $7.25 an hour, a full-time worker earns $15,080 per year before taxes. The federal poverty line for a family of two is $20,440. A full-time minimum wage worker with one child lives below the poverty line. In the richest country in the history of human civilization.

If the minimum wage had kept pace with inflation from its 1968 peak, it would be $14.42 today. If it had kept pace with productivity growth, it would be $26 an hour. Its current purchasing power is at its lowest point since 1956.

In 1980, a student could work a minimum-wage summer job and cover most of their college tuition. Today, a minimum-wage worker would need to work full-time for over 8 months just to cover in-state tuition. Before rent. Before food. Before books.

*Sources: EPI; Bureau of Labor Statistics*

## Healthcare: Spend More, Die Sooner

The United States spends $14,775 per person per year on healthcare. No country on Earth spends more. Switzerland, the next closest, spends $9,963. The OECD average, excluding the US, is $7,371.

We spend 17.2% of our entire GDP on healthcare. Germany, the next highest among wealthy nations, spends 12.3%.

What do we get for double the price?

**Life expectancy: 79 years.** The lowest among all peer wealthy nations. Comparable countries average 82.5. Japan: 84.8. Switzerland: 84.1. We pay the most and die the youngest.

**Administrative costs** are five times higher than the average wealthy country. For every doctor treating a patient, there is an army of billing specialists, insurance adjusters, and prior authorization clerks. We have built an entire industry whose purpose is to stand between sick people and healthcare.

**Maternal mortality: 17.9 deaths per 100,000 live births.** The worst in the developed world. And it gets worse by race. Black women die at a rate of 44.8 per 100,000. White women: 14.2. **Black women are 3.2 times more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes** in the richest country on the planet.

### Medical Bankruptcy

Thirty-six percent of US households carry medical debt. In 2024, 31 million adults borrowed an estimated $74 billion to cover healthcare costs. Medical bills are the number one cause of personal bankruptcy in America.

Insulin was discovered in 1921. Its inventors sold the patent for $1 each, hoping it would be accessible to everyone. Before recent manufacturer caps, a vial cost $240 retail in the US. The same vial in Canada: $30 to $40. Americans rationed it. Americans died.

Twenty-seven million Americans have no health insurance. In every other wealthy nation, that number is zero.

*Sources: OECD Health Data; CDC National Vital Statistics; Census Bureau; Yale/Lancet*

## Housing: The American Dream, Now Starting at $412,500

The median home price in 2024 was $412,500. That is up 60% since 2019. Five years. Sixty percent.

The average monthly housing payment is $3,270, which requires an annual income of $126,670. The median household income in America is roughly $80,000. Do the math. The average home is unaffordable for the average American.

The median age of a first-time homebuyer has reached 38. The highest ever recorded. Under-35 homeownership is at 36.3%, the lowest since 2019.

Over 22.7 million renter households, more than half of all renters, spend more than 30% of their income on rent. Over 12 million spend more than 50%. Among households earning under $30,000, 83% are rent-burdened.

### Homelessness: A Record High

In January 2024, 771,480 people were homeless on a single night. That is an 18% increase from 2023 and the highest number ever recorded since tracking began in 2007.

Nearly 150,000 of those people were children.

Over 42,000 were adults over 64. Almost half were unsheltered. Family homelessness surged 39% in a single year.

### Who's Buying the Houses?

In 2024, investors purchased 25% of all homes sold. Roughly 84,000 per month. Total: 1.2 million homes, a 9% increase over the previous year. These are not families buying starter homes. These are LLCs and hedge funds converting communities from places people live into assets that generate returns.

*Sources: Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies; HUD Annual Homeless Assessment Report; Census Bureau*

## Education: Debt as a Birthright

Total outstanding student debt: **$1.84 trillion.** Held by 44.6 million borrowers. Average debt per borrower: $39,547.

The average annual cost of a four-year college, adjusted for inflation: $10,231 in 1980. $28,775 in 2020. That is a 180% increase. Tuition has risen 41.7% faster than the rate of inflation in the 21st century.

For the 2025-2026 school year: $11,950 for public in-state tuition. $45,000 for private nonprofit. And that is before housing, books, food, and the general cost of being alive.

### Your ZIP Code Is Your Destiny

Over 80% of local school funding comes from property taxes. If you grow up in a wealthy neighborhood, you get a well-funded school. If you grow up in a poor one, you get crumbling buildings and teacher shortages. Per-pupil spending can vary by **$10,000 or more** between districts in the same state. That is not a funding formula. That is a caste system with extra steps.

Predominantly white districts receive billions more in funding than districts serving students of color, even with comparable student populations.

The average public school teacher salary is $72,030. Adjusted for inflation, teacher pay has declined over the past decade. In Mississippi, the average is below $50,000. We ask teachers to shape the future and pay them less than what someone makes building smartphone apps that turn your face into a cartoon cat.

*Sources: Federal Reserve; Georgetown Center on Education and the Workforce; Census Bureau*

## Food Insecurity: 14 Million Hungry Children

In 2024, 14.1 million children lived in food-insecure households. That is 18.4% of all households with children.

318,000 households experienced "very low food security" among children. That means children were hungry, skipped meals, or did not eat for a full day. In America. In 2024.

Thirty-one percent of Black households with children experienced food insecurity, compared to 14.1% of white households. 36.8% of single-mother households were food insecure.

The US GDP in 2024 was approximately $29 trillion. We are not a poor country struggling to feed its people. We are the wealthiest civilization in human history choosing not to.

And the USDA has announced it may stop publishing its annual food security report. If you stop measuring the problem, the problem disappears. On paper.

*Sources: USDA Economic Research Service*

## Taxes: A System Built for the Rich

ProPublica obtained years of IRS data on the wealthiest Americans and published what they found. The 25 richest Americans saw their wealth grow by $401 billion from 2014 to 2018. They paid $13.6 billion in federal income taxes. That is a "true tax rate" of **3.4%** against their wealth growth.

Jeff Bezos paid $0 in federal income tax in 2007 and 2011. Elon Musk paid $0 in 2018. A UC Berkeley study found Forbes 400 members paid an average effective rate of 24% from 2018 to 2020. All other taxpayers averaged 30%.

A schoolteacher pays a higher effective tax rate than a billionaire. That is not a talking point. It is an IRS data set.

### How It Works

The top ordinary income tax rate is 37%. The long-term capital gains rate is 20% (plus a 3.8% surcharge, maxing at 23.8%). The wealthiest Americans earn most of their money through investments, not salaries. So they pay the lower rate. By design.

The "carried interest loophole" allows private equity and hedge fund managers to classify their compensation as capital gains instead of ordinary income. This saves them roughly 17 percentage points in taxes plus 15.3% in self-employment taxes. Every president since Obama has promised to close it. None have.

### Corporate Taxes: The Vanishing Act

US corporate tax revenue as a share of GDP: 1.6% in 2021. The OECD average: 3.2%. In the 1950s, corporate taxes accounted for roughly 30% of federal revenue. Today: about 9%.

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act slashed the corporate rate from 35% to 21%. Permanently. The individual tax cuts for the middle class? Those were set to expire in 2025.

Here is what the TCJA actually delivered. The top 1% received average annual tax cuts of $61,090. Middle-income earners ($25K to $50K) got $359. 81% of the corporate tax cut benefits flowed to the top 10%.

The TCJA added an estimated $1.9 trillion to the national debt over 10 years. When politicians say we "can't afford" universal healthcare or free college, remember: we just gave that money away. We know exactly where it went.

*Sources: ProPublica IRS Files investigation; Tax Policy Center; Congressional Budget Office; UC Berkeley*

## Labor: The War on Workers

| Year | Union Membership Rate |
|------|----------------------|
| 1983 | 20.1% |
| 2024 | 9.9% |

Private sector union membership in 2025: 5.9%. Down from 16.5% in 1985.

The correlation is not subtle. As union membership collapsed, real wages flatlined. The average hourly wage today has roughly the same purchasing power as it did in **1978.** Forty-seven years of economic growth, and workers' real wages have not moved.

Union decline explains roughly one-third of the growth in male wage inequality since 1972 (EPI). If private-sector union density had stayed at 1979 levels, non-union men would earn an estimated $2,704 more per year. For men without a college degree: $3,172 more.

### Corporate Welfare: The Real Kind

Some of America's most profitable companies pay wages so low that their workers rely on public assistance. Which means you, the taxpayer, are subsidizing their payroll.

Walmart: approximately 14,500 employees on SNAP benefits. Roughly 29% of Nevada employees on Medicaid. The Walton family is worth over $250 billion.

Amazon: 48% of workers used some form of public assistance in a 2023 survey. Amazon's market cap exceeds $2 trillion.

70% of food stamp recipients working at large retailers were employed full-time.

These are not lazy people mooching off the system. These are people with full-time jobs at companies worth trillions of dollars whose employers have decided that the American taxpayer should cover the difference between what they pay and what it costs to survive.

*Sources: Bureau of Labor Statistics; EPI; GAO*

## Social Mobility: The American Dream Lives in Denmark Now

Economists measure social mobility using "intergenerational earnings elasticity." Higher numbers mean your parents' income more strongly determines yours. In other words: higher is worse.

| Country | Earnings Elasticity |
|---------|-------------------|
| Denmark | 0.10 to 0.20 |
| Finland | 0.17 |
| Norway | 0.16 |
| Canada | 0.20 to 0.30 |
| Germany | 0.32 |
| **United States** | **0.40 to 0.60** |

In the United States, 40 to 60% of income differences persist across generations. In Denmark, it is 10 to 20%.

The OECD estimates it takes **five generations** for a low-income American family to reach average income. In Denmark: two generations. In Norway, Finland, and Sweden: three.

The "Great Gatsby Curve," a real thing named after a fictional rich guy (which feels appropriate), shows that higher inequality correlates with lower mobility. The US sits at the extreme end of both axes. High inequality. Low mobility.

The American Dream, the idea that anyone can make it here through hard work, is statistically more achievable in Denmark, Canada, Germany, Finland, Norway, Sweden, and most of Western Europe than it is in the United States.

We kept the slogan. They kept the results.

*Sources: OECD; Chetty et al. (Harvard/Stanford mobility research)*

## The Oligarchy: 850 People, $6.2 Trillion

In 1990, there were approximately 66 American billionaires with a combined wealth of about $240 billion. In 2025, there are approximately 850 to 900, with a combined wealth exceeding **$6.2 trillion.**

Their numbers increased 13 times. Their wealth increased 25 times.

### Pandemic Profiteering

Between March 2020 and March 2024, while millions lost jobs, homes, and lives, US billionaire wealth surged 88%. Up $2.58 trillion. The number of billionaires grew from 614 to 737 in the first year alone.

One-third of all billionaire wealth gains between 1990 and 2021 occurred during 13 months of the pandemic.

Workers received $1,200 stimulus checks. Billionaires gained $2.58 trillion. To make that comparison concrete: the total value of all stimulus checks sent to all Americans during the pandemic was approximately $814 billion. Billionaires gained more than three times that amount during the same period. The stimulus was temporary. The billionaire gains were permanent.

*Sources: Forbes; Americans for Tax Fairness; Institute for Policy Studies*

|---------|---------------------|----------------------|--------------------|-----------------|-----------------| 
| Germany | Yes | Yes | 14 months | Yes | Yes |

| Canada | Yes | Low-cost | 40 weeks | Yes | Yes |
| France | Yes | Yes | 16 weeks | Yes | Yes |
| Japan | Yes | Low-cost | 12 months | Yes | Yes |

The United States is the only wealthy nation that does not guarantee paid parental leave. The only one without universal healthcare. One of the few without guaranteed paid sick days.

A single-payer healthcare system would save an estimated $450 billion per year and cover every American (Yale/Lancet study). It would save an estimated 68,000 lives annually. In 2020 alone, 212,000 Americans could have been saved.

Free public college: $28 to $75 billion per year, depending on the model. For context, the 2017 TCJA costs the Treasury roughly $190 billion per year in lost revenue. We could make public college tuition-free twice over for what we gave away in corporate tax cuts.

It is not that we cannot afford these things. It is that the people who benefit from the current system spend hundreds of millions of dollars every year to make sure we don't get them.

*Sources: OECD; Yale School of Public Health; Georgetown Center on Education and the Workforce; Tax Policy Center*

## The Racial Dimension

None of this is race-neutral.

For every $100 in wealth held by a white household, a Black household holds $15. A Hispanic household holds $22. Median net worth for white families: $284,310. For Black families: $44,100. That gap **grew** by $49,950 between 2019 and 2022, reaching $240,120. Progress is going backward.

Black women are 3.2 times more likely to die in childbirth. 31% of Black households with children are food insecure, compared to 14.1% of white households. 57% of Black renters are cost-burdened, compared to 46% of white renters. Predominantly white school districts receive billions more in funding than districts serving students of color.

These are not separate issues from economic inequality. They are the same issue, compounded by centuries of policy choices that were never fully reversed.

*Sources: Federal Reserve; CDC; USDA; Census Bureau*

## The Bottom Line

Three Americans hold more wealth than 165 million people combined. A full-time minimum wage worker cannot afford a one-bedroom apartment in any state. CEOs make 281 times their workers, a ratio that was 21 to 1 when our grandparents were building the middle class. 14 million children go hungry while billionaire wealth hit $6.2 trillion. It takes five generations to escape poverty here and two in Denmark. We spend more on healthcare than any nation and die younger. The richest Americans pay a lower tax rate than teachers. Union membership collapsed, and wages froze for 47 years. We gave $1.9 trillion in tax cuts to corporations and billionaires, then said we couldn't afford school lunches.

None of this is inevitable. None of it is natural law. Every single one of these outcomes is the result of policy choices made by people who benefit from them, funded by industries that profit from them, and sustained by a public that has been told there is no alternative.

There are alternatives. Every other wealthy country on Earth proves it every single day.

## What You Can Do

**Vote.** Not just in presidential elections. Local elections, primaries, school boards, state legislatures. The people who set tax policy, education funding, and housing rules are usually elected by turnouts under 20%. Show up.

**Follow the money.** OpenSecrets.org tracks who funds your representatives. Look up every politician you vote for. See who pays them. Then see how they vote.

**Support unions.** Whether you join one or support organizing efforts in your community, collective bargaining is the single most effective tool workers have ever had for raising wages. The correlation between union decline and wage stagnation is not subtle.

**Talk about it.** These numbers are not secret. They are just not discussed. The most powerful tool inequality has going for it is the assumption that this is just how things are. It is not. It is how things were made.

**Demand policy.** Universal healthcare. A living minimum wage. Corporate tax reform. Student debt relief. Affordable housing investment. These are not radical proposals. They are the baseline in every other wealthy democracy on Earth.

The system is working exactly as designed. It is just not designed for you.
