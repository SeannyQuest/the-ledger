# The Military Money Machine: How the Pentagon Spends Your Trillions

*Part 3 of 3: The Systemic Corruption Series*
*Daonra | The Ledger | March 2026*

---

In December 2024, the Department of Defense failed its financial audit for the eighth consecutive year. The amount of taxpayer money it could not properly account for: **$2.5 trillion**.

Let me put that in perspective. If you stacked $2.5 trillion in $100 bills, the pile would reach approximately 1,700 miles high. That's roughly the distance from New York to Denver. Now imagine losing track of that pile. And then imagine doing it eight years in a row. And then imagine nobody getting fired.

That's the Pentagon.

The Department of Defense is the only federal agency in the United States that cannot pass a financial audit. Not "struggled with." Not "had some issues." Cannot pass. Eight straight failures. The Pentagon's own auditors determined in 2023 that the department could not properly account for **63% of its $3.8 trillion in assets**. *(GAO, 2024)*

And here's the part that would be hilarious if it weren't real: in September 2001, Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld held a press conference to announce that $2.3 trillion in Pentagon transactions could not be traced. The date of that press conference? September 10, 2001. One day before the event that would justify the largest military spending increase in modern history.

That $2.3 trillion was never found. Nobody looked very hard.

## The Numbers

The United States spends more on its military than the next nine countries combined. Not the next two. Not the next five. Nine. *(PGPF, 2024)*

Here's how the spending has evolved:

- **2023:** $820.3 billion
- **2024:** $842 billion (requested)
- **2026 projection:** Exceeding **$1 trillion**

Defense spending has increased 62% in inflation-adjusted terms since 1980. *(USAFacts)*

For context, that's:
- **3.4% of GDP** on defense
- **5.4% of GDP** on education
- **2.3% of GDP** on infrastructure (among the lowest of any high-income country)

We spend more on the military than on educating our kids and fixing our roads combined. Our bridges are getting letter grades like a middle school report card, and they're not getting A's. The American Society of Civil Engineers gave U.S. infrastructure a C-minus in 2021. But sure, let's buy another aircraft carrier.

## Where the Money Goes: $2.4 Trillion to Private Contractors

From 2020 to 2024, private defense firms received **$2.4 trillion** in Pentagon contracts. That represents 54% of the entire Department of Defense discretionary budget of $4.4 trillion. More than half of what we spend on "defense" goes to private companies. *(Quincy Institute, 2025)*

The biggest players:

**Lockheed Martin** is the largest defense contractor on Earth. The F-35 program alone: $34 billion for 478 aircraft in FY2019. A $10.9 billion F-22 modernization deal. A $25.36 billion potential microelectronics contract. Lockheed Martin's revenue depends on the U.S. government deciding it needs more weapons. Fortunately for Lockheed Martin, the U.S. government always decides it needs more weapons.

**Raytheon (RTX)** runs the $11.7 billion SM-3 missile program and landed a $1.7 billion Patriot system contract in December 2025.

**Boeing** received $7.2 billion in contracts in November 2025 alone. Plus a $23.8 billion C-17 support contract. Plus $5 billion for ground-based missile defense. This is the same Boeing that can't keep door plugs on its passenger jets, but sure, let's give them $36 billion in a single year.

**Northrop Grumman** handles the B-21 Raider bomber program, nuclear weapons modernization, and surveillance systems.

## The F-35: The Most Expensive Object in Human History

If you want to understand everything wrong with military spending in a single weapons program, meet the F-35 Joint Strike Fighter.

The F-35 was supposed to be the everything fighter jet. One aircraft, three variants, for the Air Force, Navy, and Marines. A Swiss Army knife of the sky. The original pitch was efficiency: why build three different planes when you can build one?

Here's how that went:

- **Original 2001 cost estimate:** approximately $233 billion for acquisition.
- **Current acquisition cost:** approximately $485 billion. More than doubled.
- **Lifetime cost (acquisition plus sustainment):** Over **$2 trillion**.
- **Sustainment costs alone:** $1.6 trillion, a 45% increase from projections.
- **Block 4 modernization:** $6 billion over budget and 5 years behind schedule.
- **Engine and thermal issues:** Expected to add another **$38 billion** in maintenance costs.

*(GAO, ongoing; POGO)*

Two trillion dollars. For one weapons program. That's more than the GDP of Italy. It's roughly what the entire federal government spends on Medicare in five years. It's approximately $6,000 for every single person in the United States, for a plane most of them will never see.

And here's the detail that really ties the whole thing together: between 2018 and 2023, over **one million F-35 spare parts** valued at $85+ million were lost, damaged, or destroyed. The government had visibility into less than 2% of these incidents. *(GAO, 2024)*

They lost a million spare parts and only noticed 2% of the time. That's not mismanagement. That's not even negligence. I honestly don't know what to call that. If you ran a business this way, you'd go to prison for fraud. If you ran the Pentagon this way, you'd get a bigger budget next year.

## The Revolving Door: Generals for Hire

Remember how we talked about the revolving door in Part 2? The defense industry version makes the FDA look quaint.

In 2018 alone, the top 20 defense contractors hired **645 former senior government officials, military officers, members of Congress, and senior legislative staff**. Six hundred and forty-five. In a single year. *(Defense News, 2018)*

Some highlights:

- **General James Mattis:** Secretary of Defense. After leaving, joined the board of General Dynamics, one of the largest defense contractors.
- **Admiral Gary Roughead:** Chief of Naval Operations. Joined the board of Northrop Grumman.
- **Heidi Grant:** Director of the Defense Security Cooperation Agency (the office that manages foreign military sales). Left to become Boeing's lead for defense, space, and government services.

The Pentagon even has a formal program for this. It's called the **Secretary of Defense Executive Fellows (SDEF) program**. It sends active-duty military officers to work inside top defense corporations. According to a Jacobin investigation, the recommendations from SDEF fellows have consistently encouraged outsourcing more government services to private contractors.

The Pentagon sends its officers to work for defense contractors, and then those officers come back and recommend giving more work to defense contractors. This isn't a revolving door. It's a loyalty program.

## The Lobbying

Defense industry lobbying hit an all-time high of **$293.3 million** in 2025, a 25% increase from 2024. The arms industry employs 950 registered lobbyists. *(LegiStorm, OpenSecrets, 2025)*

What are they lobbying for? More contracts. Bigger budgets. Fewer audits. Less oversight.

And it works. Every time. The defense budget goes up almost every year. Both parties vote for it. If you suggested cutting the Pentagon's budget by 10%, you'd be called a threat to national security. If the Pentagon suggested accounting for 10% more of its own money, that would be historic progress.

The defense industry has perfected a strategy called "political engineering." Major weapons programs like the F-35 are deliberately spread across as many congressional districts as possible. The F-35 has suppliers in **45 states**. That means 90 senators have F-35 jobs in their states. Good luck voting to cut the program when your constituents' jobs depend on it.

It's genius, really. Build the weapon in every district, and nobody can vote against it without being accused of killing local jobs. The program doesn't even have to work. It just has to employ people.

## What We Could Buy Instead

Here's an exercise. The Pentagon's budget is $842 billion per year, projected to exceed $1 trillion. What could we do with even a fraction of that?

A 10% reallocation, about $84 billion per year, could fund:

- **Universal pre-K** for every child in America (estimated cost: $20-30 billion per year)
- **Doubling the NIH budget** for medical research ($47 billion current, so another $47 billion)
- **Fixing every structurally deficient bridge** in the country (estimated $125 billion total, so spread over 2-3 years)
- **Clean drinking water** for every American community (EPA estimates $625 billion needed over 20 years, or about $31 billion per year)

We could do all of that. And we'd still be spending more on defense than China, Russia, India, Saudi Arabia, the UK, Germany, France, and Japan combined.

But we don't. Because Lockheed Martin has 950 lobbyists and your kids' school does not.

## The Audit Question

Let's go back to those failed audits because this point deserves emphasis.

Every federal agency is required to pass a financial audit. The Pentagon has failed eight years in a row. The excuses range from "our systems are too complex" to "we're making progress." In 2024, the Pentagon's own inspector general noted improvements in some areas while acknowledging that the department still could not account for $2.5 trillion.

Try telling the IRS that your finances are "too complex" for an audit. See how that goes.

The Pentagon has the most sophisticated logistics operation in human history. It can deploy 100,000 troops to the other side of the planet in weeks. It tracks satellites in orbit. It monitors submarine movements under the ocean. But it cannot tell you where your money went.

This isn't incompetence. Incompetence at this scale, sustained for this long, requires effort. This is a system that benefits from opacity. When nobody knows where the money goes, nobody can object to how it's spent. Failed audits aren't a problem for the defense industry. They're a feature.

## What You Can Do

**1. Support defense spending transparency.** Organizations like the [Quincy Institute](https://quincyinst.org/) and [POGO](https://www.pogo.org/) push for real audit accountability and contract transparency. They fight for the radical idea that the government should know where $842 billion goes.

**2. Know your district's defense dollars.** Look up which defense contractors operate in your congressional district. Understand why your representative votes for every defense spending increase. The Department of Defense publishes contract announcements daily at defense.gov. Follow the money.

**3. Push for competitive contracting.** Many of the biggest defense programs are sole-source contracts, meaning only one company can bid. That eliminates competition and inflates costs. Demand that your representatives support open competition for defense contracts.

**4. Support the audit.** The Pentagon doesn't pass its audit because there are no consequences for failing. Advocate for legislation that ties budget increases to audit progress. No clean audit, no budget increase. It's the same standard we apply to literally every other federal agency.

**5. Question the "jobs" argument.** Yes, defense contracts create jobs. So would spending the same money on infrastructure, education, clean energy, or healthcare. The question isn't whether defense spending creates jobs. The question is whether those are the best jobs we could create with $842 billion per year. Military spending actually generates fewer jobs per dollar than investments in education, healthcare, or clean energy, according to research from UMass Amherst.

**6. Demand revolving door reform.** A 5-year ban on senior defense officials joining contractors they oversaw. No exceptions. No "advisory" loopholes. The current 1-2 year cooling-off period is a joke, and everyone involved knows it.

## The Bottom Line

The United States has built the most expensive military apparatus in human history. It spends more than the next nine countries combined. It has failed to account for trillions of dollars. Its most expensive weapons program has doubled in cost to over $2 trillion. Its oversight agencies are staffed by people who came from, and will return to, the companies they're supposed to oversee.

And every year, the budget goes up. Every year, both parties vote for it. Every year, the audits fail.

This is not about being anti-military. The men and women who serve deserve better than a system designed to enrich contractors at their expense. They deserve equipment that works, budgets that are honest, and leaders whose next career move doesn't depend on keeping Lockheed Martin happy.

This is about asking a simple question: where did the money go?

$2.5 trillion is unaccounted for. Somebody knows where it went. And whoever that somebody is, they spent $293.3 million last year making sure you don't find out.
