## 1. The Body Count

### The Numbers

Since the United States launched the "War on Terror" after September 11, 2001, the Brown University Costs of War Project estimates the total death toll at **4.5 to 4.7 million people** — and counting.

| Category | Estimated Deaths |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Direct war violence** | 897,000 – 940,000 |
| **Indirect deaths** (disease, displacement, infrastructure collapse) | 3.6 – 3.8 million |
| **Total** | **4.5 – 4.7 million** |

**Sources:** [Brown University Costs of War Project](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/findings), [Costs of War — Human Toll](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/costs/human)

### Where the Deaths Occurred

- **Iraq:** 186,901 – 210,296 documented violent civilian deaths (Iraq Body Count, through 2020). A PLOS Medicine study estimated ~461,000 "excess deaths" from 2003–2011 alone, with over 60% caused directly by violence.
- **Afghanistan:** Tens of thousands of civilians killed over 20 years of war.
- **Pakistan:** Thousands killed by drone strikes in the tribal areas.
- **Syria:** US airstrikes contributed to thousands of civilian deaths as part of the anti-ISIS campaign.
- **Yemen:** 377,000 dead by end of 2021 (direct and indirect), with over 150,000 from armed conflict.
- **Somalia:** Ongoing US airstrikes since 2007.
- **Libya:** NATO intervention in 2011 destabilized the country, contributing to ongoing civil war.

### The Indirect Deaths

The indirect death toll — **3.6 to 3.8 million people** — is the number most Americans never hear. These are people who died because:

- **Hospitals were destroyed.** In conflict zones, healthcare systems collapse. Treatable diseases become death sentences.
- **Water systems were bombed.** Cholera, dysentery, and waterborne diseases surge.
- **Food supply chains were severed.** Famine follows war like a shadow.
- **Children were the most vulnerable.** Malnutrition, lack of vaccines, contaminated water — children die at rates far exceeding combatants.

As the Costs of War Project notes: *"Death outlives war."*

**Sources:** [Costs of War — How Death Outlives War](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/papers/how-death-outlives-war), [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/6/24/how-much-have-us-wars-in-the-middle-east-and-afghanistan-cost)

## 3. Yemen

### The Worst Humanitarian Crisis in the World

Yemen has been called the worst humanitarian crisis on Earth. America's fingerprints are all over it.

### How It Started

In 2015, Saudi Arabia formed a coalition to intervene in Yemen's civil war, backed by the United States with:
- **Weapons sales:** Tens of billions in bombs, missiles, and fighter jets
- **Logistical support:** In-flight refueling for Saudi jets (until 2018)
- **Intelligence sharing:** Targeting assistance for airstrikes
- **Maintenance:** US contractors servicing Saudi aircraft

A "substantial portion" of airstrikes that killed civilians were carried out by jets **developed, sold, and maintained by US companies** and **flown by US-trained pilots.**

**Source:** [Columbia Magazine](https://magazine.columbia.edu/article/war-atrocities-yemen-linked-us-weapons)

### The Human Cost

By the end of 2021:
- **377,000 people dead** (direct and indirect causes)
- **150,000+ killed directly** by armed conflict
- **~15,000 civilians killed** by direct military action, mostly from Saudi coalition airstrikes

In 2024:
- **18.2 million Yemenis** (55% of the population) need humanitarian assistance
- **17.6 million** experiencing severe food insecurity
- **6 million** face emergency conditions — one step from famine
- **610,000 children** projected to be acutely malnourished
- **17.8 million** in need of health services
- **62% of households** reported inadequate food consumption in July 2024 — the highest ever recorded

The World Food Programme could only provide rations equaling **17% of the recommended daily intake** due to funding shortfalls.

**Sources:** [IOM Crisis Response](https://crisisresponse.iom.int/response/yemen-crisis-response-plan-2024), [ReliefWeb](https://reliefweb.int/report/yemen/yemen-humanitarian-update-issue-9-december-2024-enar), [Concern Worldwide](https://www.concern.net/news/yemen-crisis-explained)

### The Cholera Outbreak

Yemen experienced one of the largest cholera outbreaks in recorded history, with over **2.5 million suspected cases** since 2016. The destruction of water and sanitation infrastructure by coalition airstrikes created the conditions for this epidemic.

### US Direct Strikes (2024–2025)

The US didn't just arm the Saudis — it struck Yemen directly:

- **Operation Poseidon Archer** (Jan 2024 – Jan 2025): Strikes against Houthis killed **21 civilians**
- **Operation Rough Rider** (Mar – May 2025): 339 strikes, **238 civilians killed** (including 24 children), 467 injured
- **Ras Isa Port strike** (April 2025): A single strike on a fuel storage facility killed **84 civilians**

**Sources:** [Genocide Watch](https://www.genocidewatch.com/single-post/u-s-yemen-strikes-match-23-years-of-civilian-deaths), [HRW Yemen Report 2024](https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2024/country-chapters/yemen)

### The Blockade

The Saudi-led coalition enforced a naval and air blockade that restricted:
- Food
- Fuel
- Medicine

This blockade was the primary driver of the famine. When the UN Group of Eminent Experts tried to investigate, Saudi Arabia and the UAE successfully lobbied to **block the mandate** in 2021, eliminating independent monitoring.

**Source:** [ECDHR](https://www.ecdhr.org/the-impact-of-the-saudi-led-coalition-blockade-on-human-rights-in-yemen/)

**Every word was wrong.**

US intelligence agencies did not adequately vet the source. A subsequent investigation concluded the intelligence community was **"dead wrong in almost all of its pre-war judgments about Iraq's weapons of mass destruction."**

No WMDs were ever found.

1. A democratic government nationalizes resources or enacts reforms
2. An American corporation loses profits
3. The government is labeled "communist" or a "threat"
4. The CIA orchestrates a coup
5. A military dictatorship takes power
6. American corporate access is restored
7. The population suffers for decades

## 7. The Drone Program

### How It Works

The US operates a global assassination program using unmanned aerial vehicles (drones). There are two types of strikes:

1. **Personality strikes:** Targeting a specific, named individual on a "kill list"
2. **Signature strikes:** Targeting groups of people based on their behavior patterns — without knowing who they are

### The Kill List (Disposition Matrix)

Starting in 2010, the Obama administration developed the **Disposition Matrix** — a database for tracking, capturing, or killing suspected enemies. President Obama **personally approved targets** at weekly counterterrorism meetings.

The criteria for who could be killed were shaped by **John O. Brennan** (National Counterterrorism Director, later CIA Director). The legal justification: the Justice Department asserted the president could authorize killing US citizens if they posed an "imminent threat" — using a definition of "imminent" that **did not require evidence of a specific plot.**

**Sources:** [The Guardian — Kill List](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2013/jul/14/obama-secret-kill-list-disposition-matrix), [Wikipedia — Disposition Matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disposition_Matrix)

### Signature Strikes: Anonymous Killing

"Signature strikes" target people based on **"pattern of life" analysis** — essentially, being a military-age male in the wrong area doing something that looks suspicious from 10,000 feet up.

The US government classified **"military-aged males" in strike zones as combatants** unless proven otherwise — **posthumously.**

This means: if you were a young man in rural Pakistan, Yemen, or Somalia, and a drone killed you, you were counted as an enemy combatant by default. Your family had to prove you were innocent after you were already dead.

**Sources:** [CFR — Signature Strikes](https://www.cfr.org/articles/targeted-killings-and-signature-strikes), [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2016/jul/01/obama-continue-signature-strikes-drones-civilian-deaths)

### The Civilian Toll

| Source | Period | Estimated Civilian Deaths |
|--------|--------|--------------------------|
| **Bureau of Investigative Journalism** | 2002–2020 | 800 – 1,750 civilians (of 10,000–17,000 total killed) |
| **Airwars** | War on Terror total | 22,679 – 48,308 civilians |

Civilian casualty ratios vary from 7% to 15% of all drone strike deaths, depending on the source. The true number is unknown because the US government does not conduct consistent on-the-ground investigations.

**Sources:** [Bureau of Investigative Journalism](https://www.thebureauinvestigates.com/blog/2018-04-16/a-battle-for-transparency-putting-names-and-numbers-to-the-us-drone-war), [Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/biden-can-reduce-civilian-casualties-during-us-drone-strikes-heres-how/)

### The "Double Tap"

The US employed a tactic called the **"double tap"**: strike a target, wait for first responders and rescuers to arrive, then **strike again.**

This means: a bomb hits a house. Neighbors, family members, and medics rush to help the wounded. Then a second bomb hits them.

Under international humanitarian law, deliberately targeting first responders violates the principles of distinction and proportionality. When other armed groups use this tactic, the US calls it terrorism.

**Sources:** [ICCT — Double Tap Strikes](https://pt.icct.nl/article/new-light-cia-double-tap-drone-strikes-taliban-first-responders-pakistans-tribal-areas), [Harvard HPCR](https://www.hpcrresearch.org/blog/ofilio-mayorga/2013-05-08/double-tap-drone-strikes-and-denial-quarter-ihl/)

Two weeks earlier, a separate drone strike had killed his father, **Anwar al-Awlaki**, an American citizen accused of being an al-Qaeda leader. The father's killing was controversial but at least targeted.

Abdulrahman was not the target. US officials called his death **"a mistake."** He was a teenager from Denver, Colorado who had gone to Yemen looking for his father — and was killed by his own government.

In January 2017, a US raid in Yemen killed Abdulrahman's **8-year-old sister**, Nawar al-Awlaki — also an American citizen.

**Sources:** [SIPRI](https://www.sipri.org/sites/default/files/2025-04/2504_fs_milex_2024.pdf), [Peter G. Peterson Foundation](https://www.pgpf.org/article/the-united-states-spends-more-on-defense-than-the-next-9-countries-combined/)

### The Total Cost of Post-9/11 Wars

The Costs of War Project estimates the total cost at **$8 trillion** through fiscal year 2022:
- $5.8 trillion in direct costs
- $2.2 trillion in future veterans' care obligations (through 2050)

**Source:** [Brown University Costs of War](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/costs/economic)

### What Else That Money Could Buy

**Healthcare:**
- A universal single-payer healthcare system could **save** $450+ billion annually compared to the current system while covering all Americans (Yale/Lancet study)
- The US currently spends $4.5 trillion/year on healthcare — much of it on administrative overhead that other countries eliminate

**Education:**
- **Free public college tuition** for all Americans: $28–75 billion/year (depending on the model)
- Eliminate **all $1.75 trillion** in student loan debt — and still have $900+ billion left over
- Current federal education spending is a fraction of the defense budget

**Infrastructure:**
- The American Society of Civil Engineers estimates a **$3.7 trillion funding gap** in US infrastructure
- The entire defense budget for 3.7 years would close that gap completely
- $1 billion spent on healthcare creates **nearly twice as many jobs** as $1 billion spent on defense

**Sources:** [Georgetown — Free College Cost](https://cew.georgetown.edu/cew-reports/freecollegecost/), [ASCE Infrastructure Report](https://www.webuildvalue.com/en/infrastructure/us-infrastructure-resilience.html), [NIH — Military vs Social Spending](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9174441/)

## 9. Veterans

### Sent to Fight, Left to Die

The people who actually fight America's wars are, overwhelmingly, working-class Americans who joined the military for economic opportunity, college tuition, or a way out. When they come home broken, the system that sent them to war abandons them.

### Suicide

- **6,392 veterans** died by suicide in 2021 — roughly **17.5 per day**
- The widely cited figure is **"22 a day"** (from a 2013 VA report); updated VA data puts it at approximately **20 per day**
- Veteran suicide rate: **33.9 per 100,000** — 57.3% higher than non-veteran adults
- Suicide is the **second-leading cause of death** for veterans under 45

**Sources:** [VA.gov — Suicide Prevention](https://www.mentalhealth.va.gov/suicide_prevention/), [Military Times](https://www.militarytimes.com/veterans/2016/07/07/new-va-study-finds-20-veterans-commit-suicide-each-day/)

### PTSD

- **7% of all veterans** will experience PTSD in their lifetime
- Among veterans using VA healthcare: **23%** diagnosed at some point
- FY 2024: **14% of male** and **24% of female** VA patients diagnosed with PTSD
- Women veterans are more likely to have PTSD (13% lifetime vs 6% for men), partly due to **military sexual trauma**
- Combat deployment dramatically increases risk

**Source:** [VA — PTSD in Veterans](https://www.ptsd.va.gov/understand/common/common_veterans.asp)

### VA Failures

The system meant to care for veterans has repeatedly failed them:

- **2018 GAO report:** VA failed to spend millions allocated for suicide prevention, leading to a decline in outreach
- **2021 GAO report:** Suicide prevention teams experienced burnout and understaffing
- **ProPublica review (2020–present):** Systematic failures including botched screenings for suicide risk, missed mental health training, and lost track of patients
- **2023 Inspector General:** Veterans Crisis Line provided "subpar help" to a Texas veteran who later died by suicide
- Some facilities **failed to prevent suicides among patients under their direct care**

**Sources:** [Time](https://time.com/5483823/veterans-affairs-suicide-prevention/), [ProPublica](https://www.propublica.org/article/how-veterans-affairs-fails-mental-health-patients), [GovExec](https://www.govexec.com/workforce/2021/04/va-suicide-prevention-teams-report-burnout-department-fails-ensure-proper-staffing/173146/)

### Homelessness

- **32,882 veterans** experiencing homelessness in 2024 (January PIT count)
- **13,851 unsheltered**
- Veterans are **7% of all homeless adults** while being 6% of the population
- The VA housed ~48,000 homeless veterans in FY 2024, a bright spot — but the problem persists

**Source:** [VA Press Room](https://news.va.gov/press-room/veterans-homelessness-reaches-record-low-decreasing-by-7-5-since-2023/)

## 11. Sanctions

### Killing Without Bullets

US economic sanctions are presented as an alternative to war — a "peaceful" tool of foreign policy. In practice, they function as collective punishment of civilian populations.

### Iraq Sanctions (1990s)

Before the 2003 invasion, the US led comprehensive sanctions against Iraq that devastated the civilian population:

- UNICEF estimated between **400,000 and 500,000 excess deaths** among children under five between 1984 and 1999
- The under-5 child mortality rate **more than doubled** during the sanctions period
- Hospitals lacked basic medicines and equipment

In a 1996 interview on 60 Minutes, journalist Lesley Stahl asked US Ambassador to the UN **Madeleine Albright**: *"We have heard that half a million children have died. That is more children than died in Hiroshima. And, you know, is the price worth it?"*

Albright responded: **"I think that is a very hard choice, but the price, we think, the price is worth it."**

She later called the statement "a terrible mistake, hasty, clumsy and wrong."

**Sources:** [Global Policy Forum](https://archive.globalpolicy.org/component/content/article/170-sanctions/41952.html), [Democracy Now](https://www.democracynow.org/2004/7/30/democracy_now_confronts_madeline_albright_on), [NIH/PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1169290/)

### Iran

US sanctions on Iran have created a humanitarian crisis despite official "humanitarian exemptions":

- International banks and companies **over-comply** with sanctions, refusing to process legitimate medical and food transactions for fear of penalties
- Shortages of critical drugs for **epilepsy, cancer, hemophilia, and HIV**
- During COVID-19, sanctions **blocked shipments of vaccine doses**
- The UN Special Rapporteur stated sanctions have **"substantially exacerbated the humanitarian situation"**
- Disproportionate impact on those with severe diseases, disabled people, women-led households, and children

**Sources:** [HRW — Maximum Pressure](https://www.hrw.org/report/2019/10/29/maximum-pressure/us-economic-sanctions-harm-iranians-right-health), [Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/event/the-humanitarian-impact-of-us-sanctions-on-iran/)

### Cuba

The US embargo on Cuba has been in place since **1962** — over 60 years:

- Severely limited access to medical equipment, pharmaceuticals, and energy infrastructure
- Critical shortages of food, fuel, and medications
- Families forced to ration meals or go without daily nutrition
- Disrupted financial activity, increased poverty
- During COVID-19, sanctions impaired access to crucial medical supplies
- Disproportionate harm to women, children, elderly, disabled, and rural communities
- The UN General Assembly has voted to condemn the embargo **31 consecutive times** — the US (and usually only Israel) votes against

**Sources:** [Oxfam](https://webassets.oxfamamerica.org/media/documents/bp-cuba-blockade-women-250521-en.pdf), [OHCHR](https://www.ohchr.org/en/press-releases/2025/11/enforcement-and-recent-strengthening-us-sanctions-deepen-hardships-cuban)

### Venezuela

- **40,000 excess deaths** estimated between 2017–2018 (period of major sanctions escalation)
- Former UN Special Rapporteur Alfred de Zayas estimated **100,000+ deaths** from sanctions by early 2020
- **300,000+ Venezuelans** lost access to healthcare, including HIV patients, dialysis patients, and cancer patients
- Sanctions blocked transactions for purchasing **medicines, vaccines, antibiotics, and anesthetics** from abroad
- Oil revenue collapse exacerbated food crisis; undernourishment surged
- Contributed to **mass migration** (millions fled the country)

**Sources:** [CEPR](https://cepr.net/publications/rethink-sanctions-theyre-killing-as-many-people-as-war-does/), [Congressional Human Rights Commission Report](https://humanrightscommission.house.gov/sites/evo-subsites/humanrightscommission.house.gov/files/documents/20190400_CEPR_VZSanctionsCollectivePunishmt.pdf)

### The Global Picture

A study published in *The Lancet Global Health* estimated that unilateral sanctions are associated with **564,258 deaths annually** worldwide between 2010 and 2021. Children under five and individuals aged 60–80 are most affected.

Sanctions kill as many people as wars — they just do it more quietly.

**Sources:** [CEPR](https://cepr.net/publications/rethink-sanctions-theyre-killing-as-many-people-as-war-does/), [Common Dreams](https://www.commondreams.org/news/immoral-and-indefensible-study-reveals-deadly-consequences-of-us-sanctions)

## 12. What Americans Don't See

### Manufacturing Consent

In 1988, Edward S. Herman and Noam Chomsky published *Manufacturing Consent: The Political Economy of the Mass Media*, which identified five filters that shape American news coverage:

1. **Ownership:** Major media is owned by corporations whose interests align with military spending and the status quo
2. **Advertising:** Dependence on corporate advertising revenue skews coverage toward advertiser-friendly narratives
3. **Sourcing:** Media rely on government and military sources for information, which frames narratives before journalists even begin
4. **Flak:** Negative responses from powerful groups silence dissenting coverage (criticize the war? Get called unpatriotic.)
5. **Ideology:** Cold War anti-communism has been replaced by "War on Terror" framing that filters out inconvenient facts

The result: Americans are given a version of events that makes wars seem necessary, enemies seem inhuman, and alternatives seem impossible.

**Source:** Herman & Chomsky, *Manufacturing Consent* (1988)

### Embedded Journalism

During the 2003 Iraq invasion, the Pentagon introduced **embedded journalism** — placing reporters inside military units. The effects were predictable:

- Journalists developed **close bonds with troops**, leading to sympathetic coverage
- Reporters **self-censored** to maintain access and protection
- Coverage was **limited to the military perspective** — the view from the barrel of the gun, not from the bombed neighborhood
- Independent journalists who tried to report from the Iraqi civilian perspective were sometimes killed. **Al Jazeera's Baghdad bureau was struck by US missiles in 2003**, killing correspondent Tareq Ayyoub.

The lesson the Pentagon learned from Vietnam — where unrestricted coverage turned Americans against the war — was not "don't fight unjust wars." It was **"control the journalists."**

**Sources:** [Britannica — Embedded Journalism](https://www.britannica.com/topic/embedded-journalism), [PBS — Media in Wartime](https://www.pbs.org/opb/historydetectives/feature/media-in-wartime/index.html)

### The Pentagon and Hollywood

The Department of Defense maintains an **Entertainment Media Office** that offers Hollywood access to military equipment, bases, and personnel — in exchange for **script approval and storyline control.**

- Films that portray the military favorably get F-16s, aircraft carriers, and technical advisors for free
- Films that portray the military critically get **nothing** — which means they can't compete visually
- **Top Gun** (1986): The Navy insisted on changes to protect recruitment. The film boosted Navy recruitment by **500%.**
- **Top Gun: Maverick** (2022): Full Pentagon cooperation. Became a $1.5 billion recruitment ad.
- **Godzilla** (2014): Pentagon demanded removal of references to Hiroshima
- Films like **Platoon, Apocalypse Now, Full Metal Jacket** — among the greatest war films ever made — received **no military support** because they depicted war honestly

The Pentagon has influenced **thousands** of films and TV shows over more than a century. It's the largest, most sustained propaganda operation in American history — and it's funded by taxpayers.

**Sources:** [Middle East Eye](https://www.middleeasteye.net/opinion/us-pentagon-hollywood-storylines-dictates-how), [Inkstick Media](https://inkstickmedia.com/deep-dive-the-pentagon-influence-behind-the-silver-screen/), [Brown Costs of War — Militarization of Movies](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/sites/default/files/papers/Mirrlees-Militarization-of-Movies-and-TV.pdf)

### What Doesn't Get Covered

- **Civilian casualties** from US strikes receive minimal coverage. When a US drone kills a family in Yemen, it might get a brief mention. When a foreign attack kills Americans, it dominates the news cycle for weeks.
- **The aftermath of wars** disappears from coverage. Iraq, Libya, Afghanistan — once US forces leave, so do the cameras, even as civilians continue dying from the consequences.
- **Arms sales** are announced in press releases that read like product launches. The connection between "US approves $2 billion arms deal with Saudi Arabia" and "Saudi-led airstrike kills 40 children on a school bus in Yemen" is never drawn.
- **Sanctions deaths** are invisible. They don't produce dramatic footage. There's no explosion to film. People simply can't get medicine, and they die quietly.
