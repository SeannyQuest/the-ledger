## 1. The Case

### What Happened

On **January 21, 2010**, the U.S. Supreme Court issued a 5-4 decision in *Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission* that fundamentally transformed American democracy. The case originated when Citizens United, a conservative nonprofit, wanted to air a film critical of Hillary Clinton within 30 days of a primary election — something prohibited under the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (McCain-Feingold Act) of 2002.

### The Majority Opinion

Justice Anthony Kennedy, writing for the majority (joined by Roberts, Scalia, Thomas, and Alito), held that:

- **Political spending is protected speech** under the First Amendment
- The government **cannot restrict independent political expenditures** by corporations, unions, or other organizations
- Independent expenditures **do not lead to corruption** or the appearance of corruption
- Political speech is "indispensable to decision-making in a democracy," **regardless of whether it originates from a corporation or an individual**
- The government should not attempt to "equalize the relative ability of individuals and groups to influence elections"

The Court upheld disclosure and disclaimer requirements but struck down limits on independent expenditures.

### The Dissent

Justice John Paul Stevens authored a blistering 90-page dissent, joined by Ginsburg, Breyer, and Sotomayor:

> "The Court's ruling threatens to **undermine the integrity of elected institutions across the Nation.**"

Stevens argued:
- The majority showed reckless disregard for judicial precedent
- The decision misunderstood the state interests that campaign finance regulations serve
- The "anti-distortion" rationale — preventing corporate domination of political speech — was valid and essential
- **A century** of legislative and judicial efforts to limit corporate spending in elections was being swept away

### What It Overturned

The decision overturned two key precedents:

1. **Austin v. Michigan State Chamber of Commerce (1990)** — which had permitted prohibitions on independent expenditures by corporations
2. **Part of McConnell v. Federal Election Commission (2003)** — which had upheld the ban on corporations using general treasury funds for "electioneering communications"

Together with the companion case **SpeechNow.org v. FEC** (also 2010), which established that groups making only independent expenditures could raise unlimited funds, the floodgates were opened.

## 3. Super PACs

### How They Work

Super PACs (officially "independent expenditure-only committees") were born from *Citizens United* and *SpeechNow.org v. FEC*. The rules:

- **Can raise unlimited money** from individuals, corporations, and unions
- **Can spend unlimited money** to advocate for or against candidates
- **Cannot coordinate** with candidates or their campaigns (in theory)
- **Must disclose donors** (but can receive money from dark money groups, laundering the trail)

### The "Coordination" Loophole

The supposed firewall between Super PACs and campaigns is tissue-thin:

- Former staffers and close allies of candidates routinely run their Super PACs
- Campaigns post "b-roll" footage online that Super PACs can use in ads
- Candidates attend Super PAC fundraisers (but supposedly don't "ask" for specific amounts)
- Single-candidate Super PACs are functionally extensions of campaigns
- The FEC, deadlocked 3-3 along partisan lines, rarely enforces coordination rules

## 4. Dark Money

### What Is Dark Money?

Dark money is political spending where the **original source of the money is not disclosed to the public.** It flows primarily through:

- **501(c)(4) "social welfare" organizations** — Not required to reveal donors
- **501(c)(6) trade associations** — Business leagues that can engage in politics
- **Shell LLCs** — Vaguely named entities that funnel money to Super PACs

### How 501(c)(4)s Hide Donors

- Classified as "social welfare" organizations under the tax code
- Can receive **unlimited donations** from corporations, individuals, and unions
- Political activity cannot be their "primary purpose" — but this rule is vaguely defined and rarely enforced
- The **IRS**, not the FEC, oversees them — and the IRS has largely abdicated this role
- In **2018**, the Treasury Department announced that 501(c)(4)s would **no longer be required to identify substantial donors** on their tax filings

### The Biggest Dark Money Groups

#### Conservative

| Organization | Key Details |
|---|---|
| **Crossroads GPS** | Co-founded by Karl Rove; gained 501(c)(4) status in 2015 after a 5-year wait; spent tens of millions on elections while keeping donors secret; channeled funds to other politically active nonprofits |
| **Americans for Prosperity (AFP)** | Koch brothers' flagship organization; founded 2004; spent millions fighting the ACA, climate regulation, and donor disclosure laws; one of the most influential political organizations in modern history |
| **U.S. Chamber of Commerce** | Spent $100M+ on elections since 2010; receives corporate dues that fund political activity without attribution |
| **Judicial Crisis Network** | Spent millions on Supreme Court confirmation battles (Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett) from undisclosed donors |

#### Liberal

| Organization | Key Details |
|---|---|
| **Sixteen Thirty Fund** | "The indispensable heavyweight of Democratic dark money"; acts as a fiscal sponsor for progressive projects; spent nearly **$410 million in the 2020 cycle alone**; channeled hundreds of millions into abortion rights, voting rights, and climate causes |
| **Arabella Advisors network** | Manages multiple dark money nonprofits (Sixteen Thirty Fund, New Venture Fund, Hopewell Fund); combined spending in the billions |
| **Majority Forward** | Linked to Senate Majority Leader's political operation |

### The Shell Company Pipeline

Shell companies and undisclosed nonprofits injected **$1.3 billion** into Super PACs in the 2024 election cycle alone. These vaguely named LLCs make it nearly impossible to trace money to its original source.

## 5. Corporate Capture: Buying Policy Outcomes

### Pharmaceutical Industry

**Lobbying spend:** $387 million in 2024 alone. The health sector overall: $750 million.

**The results:**
- Americans pay the **highest drug prices in the world** — by far
- Merck's Keytruda: **$191,000/year** in the U.S. (2022) vs. **$89,000** in Germany
- Eliquis: price **more than doubled** in the U.S. to $7,100; in Japan: **$900**
- For decades, the industry **blocked Medicare from negotiating drug prices** — Congress only partially addressed this in 2022 with the Inflation Reduction Act, and only for 10 drugs
- PhRMA alone spent **$31 million** lobbying in 2024
- An FDA panel cardiologist who voted to approve Brilinta later received **over $200,000** from manufacturer AstraZeneca

### Fossil Fuel Industry

**Lobbying spend:** $150M+ in 2024 federal lobbying; $243 million lobbying Congress in 2023-2024 combined.

**The results:**
- The U.S. provides an estimated **$20 billion annually** in direct subsidies and tax benefits to fossil fuel companies
- Fossil fuel groups contributed **$96 million** to Trump's 2024 campaign and affiliated PACs
- The industry secured **€17 billion** in public subsidies for carbon capture technology — which primarily enables continued extraction
- Oil and gas companies spent a record **$38 million** on California state-level lobbying in 2024

### Financial Sector

**The results:**
- Wall Street lobbying contributed to the deregulation that caused the **2008 financial crisis**
- Before the Volcker Rule was even written, Wall Street held **419 meetings** with federal regulators — **93% of all external meetings** on the rule
- The financial industry spent over **$2 billion** to influence decision-making during the 2016 election cycle

### Big Tech

**Combined lobbying (Google, Amazon, Apple, Meta):** $51.7M in 2020; nearly $70M in 2021; $95M in 2021-2022 to kill antitrust bills.

**The results:**
- Meta spent a record **$8 million** in Q1 2025 alone — ahead of its FTC antitrust trial
- Amazon spent **$108 million+** on lobbying over five years
- Apple spent a record **$9.3 million** in 2022 fighting antitrust legislation
- Despite bipartisan support, the **American Innovation and Choice Online Act** (AICOA) was killed — largely attributed to tech industry lobbying
- No major federal antitrust legislation has passed despite bipartisan public support

### Total Lobbying Industry

- **2024:** Federal lobbying hit a record **$4.4 billion**
- **2023:** A record **$4.3 billion** in federal lobbying; state and federal combined topped **$46 billion** since 2015
- **13,000+ registered federal lobbyists** in 2024 — a record
- Special interest groups spent **$4.2 billion** lobbying federal lawmakers in 2023 alone

## 6. The Revolving Door

### The Numbers

- **1970s:** Less than **5%** of retiring legislators became lobbyists
- **1998-2004:** **43%** of members leaving Congress became lobbyists (42% of House members, **50% of senators**)
- **2016 study:** **25%** of House members and **29%** of Senators who left Congress between 1976-2012 registered as lobbyists — the percentage steadily increasing
- **2019 study:** **59%** of representatives who leave Congress for the private sector join lobbying or political consulting firms
- **Since 2011:** At least **176 former members of Congress** have become lobbyists or taken influence roles
- Today: roughly **half of retiring senators** and a **third of retiring House members** register as lobbyists

- The period doesn't even cover a full congressional session
- Many use titles like "strategic adviser" to sidestep the rules entirely

## 7. Foreign Money

### The Vulnerability

While federal law prohibits foreign nationals and governments from spending money in U.S. candidate elections, **Citizens United blew open massive loopholes:**

### How Foreign Money Gets In

1. **U.S.-incorporated subsidiaries of foreign corporations** can make unlimited political expenditures — even if the parent company is 100% foreign-owned
2. **Shell LLCs** can funnel money to Super PACs with no requirement to prove the funds aren't foreign
3. **501(c)(4) organizations** can receive foreign donations and then spend on elections — the original source is never disclosed
4. **Ballot measure campaigns** at the state and local level can legally accept foreign contributions (per FEC rules)
5. **Dark money networks** provide layers of separation that make tracing foreign funds virtually impossible

### The Scale

- Shell companies and undisclosed nonprofits injected **$1.3 billion** into Super PACs in 2024
- Without transparency requirements, it is **impossible to determine** how much of this originated from foreign sources
- The FEC, deadlocked along partisan lines, has consistently failed to address these loopholes

### Legislative Attempts

- **"Get Foreign Money Out of U.S. Elections Act"** — introduced but not passed
- **"Preventing Foreign Interference in American Elections Act"** — introduced but not passed
- Both aimed to establish foreign ownership thresholds and prevent funneling through intermediaries

As Justice Stevens warned in his dissent: the majority's framework provides **no mechanism** to prevent foreign corporations from using U.S. subsidiaries to influence American elections.

**Sources:** [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/topics/money-politics/foreign-spending), [Center for American Progress](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/citizens-united-paves-way-foreign-governments-influence-u-s-elections/), [Issue One](https://issueone.org/articles/election-security-at-risk-5-loopholes-that-allow-foreign-interference-in-our-elections/), [Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org), [Harvard Law](https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2019/12/06/ending-foreign-influenced-corporate-spending-in-u-s-elections/)

## 8. State-Level Damage

### Captured Legislatures

Dark money at the state level flows through 501(c)(4)s, trade associations, and shell LLCs — the same playbook as federal elections, but with even weaker disclosure laws.

#### The Ohio Nuclear Bailout Scandal

The most vivid example of state-level dark money corruption:

- Energy company **FirstEnergy** funneled **$60 million** through a dark money group
- The money was linked to **Ohio House Speaker Larry Householder**
- The scheme secured a **$1 billion bailout** for failing nuclear power plants (HB 6)
- Householder was convicted in a **federal racketeering case** — the largest corruption scandal in Ohio history
- A federal judge called dark money a "breeding ground for corruption"

### Judicial Elections

Dark money in judicial elections poses an existential threat to court independence:

- Unlimited spending by special interest groups has funded costly TV ad campaigns, character assassinations, and misinformation in judicial races
- Studies demonstrate that **substantial campaign spending influences judicial decision-making** in both civil and criminal cases
- States severely affected: **North Carolina, Alabama, Arkansas, Tennessee, Montana, Texas, Illinois, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Mississippi, Louisiana, West Virginia, and Florida**
- The **Judicial Crisis Network** spent millions on Supreme Court confirmations from entirely undisclosed donors — the same dark money networks operate at the state level
- A significant majority of Americans now believe **partisan politics and ideological leanings influence court decisions** — a perception driven by dark money in judicial contests

### The Pattern

1. Dark money group funds attack ads against state legislators or judges
2. Funded candidates win and enact favorable legislation or rulings
3. The public never learns who paid for the outcome
4. Repeat

**Sources:** [American Bar Association](https://www.americanbar.org), [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org/topics/courts/promote-fair-state-courts/money-judicial-elections), [Common Cause Ohio](https://www.commoncause.org/ohio/), [Citizens for Ethics](https://www.citizensforethics.org)

## 9. What Other Countries Do

### The Comparison

| Country | Spending Limits | Corporate Donations | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **United States** | No limits on independent expenditures; candidates can spend unlimited personal funds | Unlimited through Super PACs/dark money | Weakest major democracy on campaign finance |
| **United Kingdom** | Parties: ~£35.1M; Candidates: ~£17,000-£20,000; Third parties: £702,130 | Permitted, but transparent | Strict spending caps; regulated campaign periods |
| **Canada** | Parties: ~$30M; Candidates: $89K-$153K | **Banned since 2007** (corporations and unions) | Individual limit: $1,750/year; most restrictive in Anglosphere |
| **France** | Deputies: €38,000 + €0.15/inhabitant | **Banned since 1995** | Public reimbursement for qualifying candidates; foreign donations banned |
| **Germany** | No spending limits | No contribution limits (except anonymous >€500) | Strong public funding system; mandatory disclosure >€10,000; foreign corp donations banned |
| **Sweden** | No spending limits | No donation limits | Foreign donations banned; parties report income annually |
| **Norway** | No spending limits | Foreign and anonymous donations banned | Government-partially-owned corps can't donate |
| **Finland** | Individual donations capped at €30,000/year | Regulated | Must declare donations ≥€1,500 |

### What Stands Out

- **Canada and France ban corporate donations entirely** — and their democracies function
- The **UK caps total party spending** at £35.1M — the U.S. spent **$15.9 billion** in 2024
- **France bans foreign donations** of any kind — the U.S. can't even verify if foreign money is in the system
- Multiple countries provide **public financing** to reduce dependence on private donors
- The U.S. is the **only major democracy** that allows unlimited anonymous spending to influence elections

### The Math

If you applied UK-style spending limits to U.S. elections, total spending would drop by roughly **99%**.

## 10. Reform Efforts: Tried, Blocked, Stalled

### The DISCLOSE Act

**What it does:** Requires Super PACs and dark money organizations to promptly disclose donors who contribute more than $10,000. Prevents laundering campaign funds through front groups.

**Status:**
- First introduced in **2010** as a direct response to Citizens United
- **Passed the House** in June 2010
- **Killed by Republican filibuster** in the Senate
- Reintroduced in every subsequent Congress
- Blocked every single time

### The For the People Act (H.R. 1 / S. 1)

**What it does:** Comprehensive reform — expands voting rights, reforms campaign finance, ends gerrymandering, establishes ethics rules for federal officeholders. Includes DISCLOSE Act provisions and expresses support for a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United.

**Status:**
- Passed the House in **2019** and **2021**
- **Killed by Senate filibuster** both times
- Would have been the most significant democracy reform in decades

### Constitutional Amendments

Several have been proposed:

- **Democracy for All Amendment** (Sen. Jeanne Shaheen) — empowers Congress and states to set reasonable campaign finance rules
- **We the People Amendment** (Rep. Pramila Jayapal) — states that constitutional rights are for people, not corporations; ends corporate personhood
- **Citizens Over Corporations Amendment** (Rep. Summer Lee, Sen. Adam Schiff, 2025) — restores Congress's authority to limit political spending

**Status:** None have come close to the 2/3 supermajority needed in both chambers, plus ratification by 3/4 of state legislatures.

### Who Blocks Reform?

- **Senate Republicans** have filibustered every major campaign finance reform bill since 2010
- **The U.S. Chamber of Commerce** lobbies aggressively against disclosure requirements
- **Americans for Prosperity (Koch network)** has actively campaigned against donor disclosure laws
- **The FEC itself** is structurally broken — deadlocked 3-3 along partisan lines, it rarely enforces existing rules
- **The Supreme Court** — the conservative majority that decided Citizens United continues to strike down campaign finance regulations

### State-Level Bright Spots

- **Arizona** enacted a dark money disclosure law (though it faces legal challenges)
- **Oregon** passed transparency measures
- **22 states and 800+ municipalities** have called for a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United

**Sources:** [Brennan Center](https://www.brennancenter.org), [Campaign Legal Center](https://campaignlegal.org), [Public Citizen](https://www.citizen.org), [American Promise](https://americanpromise.net), Congressional records

## The Bottom Line

In 2010, five Supreme Court justices decided that corporations have the same free speech rights as people, and that spending money is speech. In doing so, they:

- **Overturned a century** of campaign finance law
- **Enabled unlimited anonymous spending** in elections
- **Created a system** where a handful of billionaires can outspend millions of citizens
- **Opened the door** to foreign money flowing through shell corporations
- **Gutted enforcement** by empowering dark money groups the FEC can't regulate

The result? A political system where:

- **85% of Americans** know it's a money-driven game only the wealthy can play
- **82%** see money in politics as a threat to democracy
