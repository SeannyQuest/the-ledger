## 1. Legal Corruption: The Lobbying Machine

### The Scale

Federal lobbying spending has exploded to record levels:

- **2024:** $4.4–$4.5 billion (record at the time) — *OpenSecrets, Bloomberg Government, LegiStorm*
- **2025:** $5.24 billion — a $768 million increase from 2024, the largest year-over-year jump ever recorded — *LegiStorm*
- **Q1 2025 alone:** $1.17 billion in federal lobbying expenditures — *Quorum*

For context: there are 535 members of Congress. That's roughly **$9.8 million in lobbying spending per member of Congress per year**.

### How It Works: Legal Bribery

The system operates through several mechanisms that effectively legalize what would be considered corruption in most countries:

**Direct Lobbying:** Companies and industries hire lobbyists — often former members of Congress or their staff — to meet with legislators, draft legislation, and push for favorable policy. There are approximately **12,000 registered federal lobbyists**, roughly 22 per member of Congress.

**Campaign Contributions:** While direct corporate contributions to candidates are technically banned, the system is built on workarounds:

- **Super PACs** spent **$2.7 billion** in the 2024 election cycle — *Brennan Center*
- **Dark money** hit a record **$1.9 billion** in 2024, nearly doubling the prior record of $1 billion in 2020 — *Brennan Center*
- Total 2024 federal election spending: at least **$15.9 billion** — *OpenSecrets*

**Citizens United v. FEC (2010):** The Supreme Court ruling that opened the floodgates by declaring corporate spending on elections is protected "free speech." Combined with *McCutcheon v. FEC* (2014), which struck down aggregate contribution limits, these decisions dismantled most meaningful campaign finance regulation.

**The Revolving Door:** Lobbyists become staffers become lobbyists. The knowledge of how government works *is* the product being sold.

## 2. Congressional Stock Trading

### The STOCK Act: A Law With No Teeth

The Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge (STOCK) Act, passed in 2012, was supposed to prevent members of Congress from trading on inside information. In practice:

- **Standard penalty for disclosure violations:** $200
- **That penalty can be waived** by the Ethics Committee
- **Dozens of members** have violated it with essentially zero consequences
- There is **no independent enforcement mechanism** — Congress polices itself

## 3. Regulatory Capture

Regulatory capture occurs when the agencies created to protect the public instead serve the industries they're supposed to regulate. It is the norm, not the exception, across major U.S. regulatory agencies.

### FDA ↔ Pharmaceutical Industry

**The Revolving Door:**
- **9 of the last 10 FDA Commissioners** went on to work for the pharmaceutical industry or serve on drug company boards — *Public Citizen*
- **Dr. Scott Gottlieb**, former FDA Commissioner, joined **Pfizer's board of directors** less than 3 months after leaving the agency
- A 2016 study found that **15 of 26 FDA hematology-oncology reviewers** who left the agency went to work for the companies they had regulated — *Stanford Law*
- **11 of 16 FDA drug reviewers** who worked on 28 specific drug approvals took positions at companies they had recently regulated — *BMJ*

**Consequences:**
- **Aduhelm (Alzheimer's drug):** Approved despite both pivotal trials being stopped for futility, the FDA's own statistical reviewer opposing approval, and an advisory committee voting overwhelmingly against it. Priced at **$56,000/year per patient.** — *Cambridge University Press*
- The FDA approves drugs funded by the industry it regulates: **75% of FDA's drug review budget** comes from pharmaceutical company user fees (PDUFA)

### FAA ↔ Boeing

**Self-Regulation:**
- The FAA **delegated safety certification** to Boeing itself, allowing the company to essentially regulate its own aircraft
- Boeing **helped craft a bill** that weakened government oversight of new airplane designs — it became law **weeks before** the first 737 MAX crash — *POGO*

**The Result:**
- **Two 737 MAX crashes (2018–2019):** 346 people killed
- Investigations revealed the FAA had handed "**nearly complete control to Boeing**" during the 737 MAX certification
- The FAA failed to independently assess the MCAS automated system that caused both crashes
- **January 2024:** A door plug blew out of an Alaska Airlines 737 MAX 9 mid-flight — revealing continued quality control failures

**The Revolving Door:**
- A former **top FAA safety official who oversaw Boeing** later took a leading role at a major aviation industry lobby **funded in part by Boeing** — *Aviation Pros*
- FAA-deputized Boeing employees reported feeling **pressured to act in Boeing's interest** and feared retaliation for raising safety concerns

### SEC ↔ Wall Street

**The Revolving Door:**
- Between 2006–2010, **219 former SEC employees** sought to represent clients before the SEC — some filing within **days** of leaving — *POGO*
- Former SEC employees routinely help corporations **counter investigations, soften enforcement actions, and gain exemptions from federal law** — *Bill Moyers*
- An SEC Commissioner with prior investment firm experience **helped derail tightening of money market fund regulation** in 2012 — *Harvard Law*

**Enforcement Failures:**
- The SEC **failed to act on repeated warnings** about Allen Stanford's Ponzi scheme for **over a decade**
- **Harry Markopolos** tried for **10 years** to get the SEC to investigate Bernie Madoff — he called the agency "**nonfunctional, captive to the industry**"
- After the 2008 crash, the SEC negotiated **reduced charges** with banks that had multiple fraud violations — *Brookings*

### FCC ↔ Telecoms

**The Revolving Door:**
- **Ajit Pai:** Former Verizon attorney → FCC Chairman → repealed net neutrality (a massive win for ISPs like Comcast and AT&T)
- **Meredith Attwell Baker:** Voted to approve the Comcast-NBC Universal merger → **took a job at Comcast 4 months later**
- **Michael Powell:** FCC Chairman → became chief of the cable industry's **largest lobbying operation** (NCTA)
- **Dorothy Attwood:** Left the FCC's Wireline Competition Bureau → became SVP at AT&T, **helping reshape rules she had drafted** — *Center for Public Integrity*

### EPA ↔ Fossil Fuels

**Industry Takeover:**
- A report found **43 of 111 nominees/appointees** to agencies like EPA and Interior had **direct ties to the fossil fuel industry** — *Corporate Knights*
- In 2025, **all top EPA management positions** were filled by former lobbyists and lawyers from regulated industries — *ToxinFreeUSA*
- EPA leadership included a politician with **over $400,000 in oil and gas industry backing**
- Under this leadership: formaldehyde limits were **doubled**, PFAS drinking water limits were **rescinded**, toxic pesticide approvals were **fast-tracked**

## 4. Military-Industrial Complex

### The Numbers

**U.S. Defense Spending:**
- **2023:** $820.3 billion (13.3% of federal budget)
- **2024 request:** $842 billion
- **2026 projection:** Exceeding **$1 trillion**
- The U.S. spends **more on defense than the next 9 countries combined**
- Defense spending has increased **62% (inflation-adjusted) since 1980** — *PGPF, USAFacts*

**As Percentage of GDP (2023):**
- Defense: **3.4%**
- Healthcare: **17.6%** (mostly private spending — the government doesn't get this for its citizens; it pays for a broken system)
- Education: **5.4%**
- Infrastructure: **2.3%** (among the lowest of high-income countries)

### The Contractors

From 2020–2024, private firms received **$2.4 trillion** in Pentagon contracts — representing **54% of the department's total discretionary spending** of $4.4 trillion — *Quincy Institute*

**Major Players:**
- **Lockheed Martin:** Largest defense contractor. F-35 program alone: **$34 billion** contract for 478 aircraft (FY2019). $10.9 billion F-22 modernization. $25.36 billion potential microelectronics contract.
- **Raytheon (RTX):** $11.7 billion SM-3 missile program. $1.7 billion Patriot system contract (Dec 2025).
- **Boeing:** $7.2 billion in contracts (Nov 2025 alone). $23.8 billion C-17 support contract. $5 billion GMD missile defense.
- **Northrop Grumman:** B-21 Raider bomber program, nuclear weapons modernization, surveillance systems.

### The F-35: A Case Study in Waste

The F-35 Joint Strike Fighter is the **most expensive weapons program in human history:**

- **Original 2001 cost estimate:** ~$233 billion for acquisition
- **Current acquisition cost:** ~$485 billion (more than doubled)
- **Lifetime cost (acquisition + sustainment):** Over **$2 trillion**
- **Sustainment costs alone:** $1.6 trillion (45% increase from projections)
- **Block 4 modernization:** $6 billion over budget and 5 years behind schedule
- **Engine/thermal issues:** Expected to add **$38 billion** in maintenance costs
- Between 2018–2023, over **1 million F-35 spare parts** valued at $85+ million were **lost, damaged, or destroyed** — the government had visibility into less than 2% of these incidents — *GAO, POGO*

### Pentagon Audit Failures

The Department of Defense has **failed its financial audit for 8 consecutive years** — the only federal agency that cannot pass one.

- **December 2024:** $2.5 trillion in taxpayer funds reported as unaccounted for
- **December 2023:** Pentagon could not properly account for **63% of its $3.8 trillion in assets**
- **2001:** Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld stated that **$2.3 trillion** in Pentagon transactions could not be traced — announced September 10, 2001 (the day before 9/11)

### The Revolving Door

- In **2018 alone**, the top 20 defense contractors hired **645 former senior government officials, military officers, members of Congress, and senior legislative staff** — *Defense News*
- **Gen. James Mattis:** Secretary of Defense → joined the board of **General Dynamics**
- **Admiral Gary Roughead:** Chief of Naval Operations → board of **Northrop Grumman**
- **Heidi Grant:** Director of the Defense Security Cooperation Agency → **Boeing's lead** for defense, space, and government services
- The Pentagon's own **Secretary of Defense Executive Fellows (SDEF) program** sends military officers to work for top defense corporations — their recommendations have consistently encouraged **outsourcing services to private contractors** — *Jacobin*

## 5. The Tax System

### Billionaire Tax Rates: The ProPublica IRS Files

In June 2021, ProPublica published leaked IRS data (obtained by contractor Charles Littlejohn, now serving a 5-year sentence) revealing what the wealthiest Americans actually pay in taxes:

**The "True Tax Rate" (taxes paid vs. wealth growth):**

| Billionaire | Wealth Growth (2014–2018) | Federal Income Tax Paid | "True Tax Rate" |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Warren Buffett** | $24.3 billion | $125 million | **0.1%** |
| **Jeff Bezos** | $99 billion | $973 million | **~1%** |
| **Elon Musk** | $13.9 billion | $455 million | **3.3%** |
| **Michael Bloomberg** | $22.5 billion | $292 million | **1.3%** |

- The **25 wealthiest Americans** saw their wealth grow by **$401 billion** (2014–2018) but paid only **$13.6 billion** in federal income taxes = **3.4% "true tax rate"**
- **Jeff Bezos paid $0** in federal income tax in 2007 and 2011
- **Elon Musk paid $0** in federal income tax in 2018
- A single worker earning **$45,000/year** often pays a higher effective tax rate than billionaires — *ProPublica*

**NBER Study Confirmation:**
- The top 0.0002% of households paid an average total effective tax rate of **24%** (2018–2020)
- The overall U.S. population average: **30%**
- When measured against wealth, the richest Americans paid only **1.3%** of their holdings in taxes annually — *National Bureau of Economic Research*

### How They Do It

Billionaires minimize taxes through entirely legal strategies:
- **"Buy, Borrow, Die":** Accumulate assets (taxed at 0% until sold), borrow against them for living expenses (loans aren't income), die and pass assets to heirs with a stepped-up cost basis (erasing all capital gains)
- **Capital gains vs. income:** Investment income taxed at max **20%**, while wages are taxed up to **37%** — plus payroll taxes
- **Charitable deductions:** Donate appreciated assets (avoid capital gains tax AND get a deduction)
- **Opportunity zones, real estate depreciation, carried interest loopholes**

### Corporate Tax Avoidance

**The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act:**
- Dropped the statutory corporate rate from **35% to 21%**
- **296 large, consistently profitable corporations** saw their average effective tax rate fall from **22.0% (2013–2016) to 12.8% (2018–2021)** — *ITEP*
- These companies paid **$240 billion less in taxes** from 2018–2021 than they would have under pre-2017 rates

**Offshore Havens:**
- Fortune 500 companies held a record **$2.6 trillion in profits offshore** (2016–2017), avoiding an estimated **$767 billion** in U.S. federal income taxes — *ITEP*
- **73% of Fortune 500 corporations** (366 companies) use offshore tax havens
- Estimated annual U.S. revenue lost to offshore profit shifting: **$94–$135 billion** — *Duke University, IMF*
- From 2016–2021, the U.S. alone lost over **$271 billion** from multinational profit shifting — *Tax Justice Network*

**Effective Tax Rates of Major Corporations:**
- In 2018, 379 profitable Fortune 500 companies paid an average effective rate of **11.3%** — barely half the statutory 21%
- **General Electric, General Motors, Meta, Tesla, T-Mobile** collectively paid **6.9%** on over $70 billion in profits
- The number of companies paying under 10% effective tax rose from **56 to 95** after the 2017 tax law

### Who Wrote the Tax Code?

The tax code wasn't written by accident. It was written by lobbyists:
- **Tax lobbying** is one of the most lucrative sectors in Washington
- The 2017 tax law was drafted with **heavy input from corporate lobbyists**, many of whom helped write the specific provisions their clients would benefit from
- Major beneficiaries of the law (like the Koch network) spent **hundreds of millions** lobbying for its passage

## 6. Healthcare Corruption

### The Price Tag

The U.S. healthcare system is the most expensive in the world — by a massive margin — with outcomes that trail peer nations:

- **2024 per capita spending:** $14,885 (U.S.) vs. $9,963 (Switzerland, #2) vs. $7,371 (OECD average excluding U.S.) — *KFF, Commonwealth Fund*
- **As % of GDP:** 17.6% (nearly double the peer country average of ~11%)
- **Administrative costs:** Over $1,000 per person — **5x more** than the average of other wealthy countries — *Johns Hopkins*

### Outcomes That Don't Match the Price

Despite spending 2x more than peer nations:
- **Lower life expectancy** than comparable countries
- **Higher maternal mortality** — worst among wealthy nations
- **Higher infant mortality**
- **Fewer physicians per capita**
- **Fewer hospital beds per capita**
- Americans report more difficulty affording healthcare than citizens of any other wealthy democracy — *Commonwealth Fund*

### Pharma Lobbying

The pharmaceutical industry is the **#1 lobbying spender** in Washington:
- **2024:** $388 million in federal lobbying
- **2025 (first half):** $227 million — on pace to shatter the record
- **PhRMA** (the industry's main trade group): $31 million (2024), $37.9 million (2025)
- PhRMA funnels millions to **dark-money groups** like the American Action Network, which supports candidates who protect pharma pricing — *Sludge, Jacobin*

### Insurance Company Profits

The 7 largest publicly traded U.S. health insurance companies:
- **2024 collective profits:** $71.3 billion
- **2024 total revenues:** $1.517 trillion (nearly **tripled** from $511 billion in 2014)
- **2025 total revenues:** Nearly $1.7 trillion; profits over $54 billion
- These profits are generated partly through **raising premiums** and **overcharging the government** through Medicare Advantage upcoding and fraud — *Common Dreams, Epic for America*

### Who Blocks Medicare for All

The private health insurance industry and its corporate allies fund opposition to single-payer healthcare to preserve their profit model. The top recipients of health insurance and pharma contributions are consistently those who vote against universal coverage.

Key opposition tactics:
- Fund think tanks and advocacy groups to produce anti-M4A messaging
- Run ads warning about "government takeover of healthcare"
- Contribute heavily to members of relevant committees (Finance, Ways & Means, HELP)

## 7. Housing & Wall Street

### The 2008 Financial Crisis: Almost Nobody Went to Jail

The 2008 crash was caused by systemic fraud in the mortgage industry — fraudulent lending, fraudulent bundling of mortgages into securities, fraudulent ratings of those securities, and fraudulent insurance (AIG). The result:

- **9 million Americans** lost their homes
- **$11 trillion** in household wealth evaporated
- **8.7 million jobs** lost
- The government bailed out Wall Street with **$700 billion** (TARP) plus trillions in Federal Reserve emergency lending

**Who went to jail:**
- **Kareem Serageldin** — a mid-level Credit Suisse executive — is the **only banker in the United States** sentenced to prison as a result of the 2008 crisis. He was convicted for mismarking bond prices to hide losses. — *Wikipedia, Marketplace*

For comparison: after the **Savings & Loan crisis** of the 1980s–90s (a much smaller scandal), over **1,000 bankers** were convicted and jailed. — *Bill Moyers*

**Why no prosecutions?** Former prosecutors cite a "perceived absence of commitment, competence, and courage" by political leaders within the Department of Justice. Financial institutions paid **billions in fines** — but those fines came from **shareholders**, not the individuals who committed fraud.

### Institutional Home Buying

After the 2008 crash, Wall Street firms pivoted from crashing the housing market to **buying it up:**

- **Blackstone** (not BlackRock — commonly confused) created **Invitation Homes** in 2012 to acquire foreclosed properties, amassing tens of thousands of rental homes
- Institutional investors now own an estimated **1–3.8%** of total single-family housing stock nationally — but their purchases are **concentrated in specific markets**, particularly Sun Belt metros — *Brookings, Realtor.com*
- These firms **outbid individual homebuyers** with all-cash offers
- They convert **starter homes into rentals**, destroying the traditional path to homeownership and generational wealth
- Corporate landlords have been documented charging **higher rents** and filing **more evictions** than smaller landlords — *HUD*
- Purchases are disproportionately concentrated in **low-income and historically non-white neighborhoods**

### The Wealth Gap in Housing

- Homeownership is the **primary wealth-building tool** for middle-class Americans
- **White homeownership rate:** ~74%
- **Black homeownership rate:** ~44%
- The racial homeownership gap is **wider today than when the Fair Housing Act was passed in 1968**

## 9. Wealth Inequality

### The Divide

**As of Q1 2024:**
- The **top 1%** of U.S. households hold **30.5%** of total national wealth
- The **bottom 50%** hold just **2.5%**
- The top 1% has seen wealth grow by **~300% since 1989**
- The bottom 50%'s share of wealth **declined 26.1%** (inflation-adjusted) over the same period — *Federal Reserve*

**Billionaire Growth:**
- U.S. billionaires' collective wealth: **$8.1 trillion** (2025) — *Common Dreams*
- During COVID (2020), billionaire wealth **increased by 70%** — the steepest single-year increase on record
- **735 billionaires** possess more wealth than the **bottom half of all U.S. households** (as of late 2022)
- The U.S. has over **850 billionaires** (2024), commanding 33% of the world's billionaires — *Henley & Partners*

### CEO Pay vs. Worker Pay

The ratio tells the story of a system designed to funnel wealth upward:

| Year | CEO-to-Worker Pay Ratio |
|---|---|
| **1965** | 21:1 |
| **1989** | 61:1 |
| **2000** | 366:1 (peak during dot-com) |
| **2020** | 351:1 |
| **2024** | **281:1** |

- Average S&P 500 CEO compensation in 2024: **$18.9 million** — *EPI*
- From 1978–2024: CEO compensation grew **1,094%**. Typical worker compensation grew **26%.** — *Economic Policy Institute*
- Among the "Low-Wage 100" S&P 500 firms, the ratio is **632:1** (2024)

### Productivity vs. Wages: The Great Decoupling

This is perhaps the single most important economic chart in American history:

- **1948–1979:** Productivity grew **108%**. Wages grew **93%**. Workers shared in growth.
- **1979–2024:** Productivity grew **80.5%**. Worker compensation grew only **26%**. — *EPI*
- The gap between productivity and pay represents **trillions of dollars** in value created by workers but captured by owners and executives
- Key drivers: de-unionization, globalization, automation, policy choices that favored capital over labor — *World Economic Forum*

### What Changed?

The divergence began in the late 1970s–early 1980s and accelerated through:
- **Union membership decline:** From ~35% in the 1950s to ~10% today
- **Tax policy:** Top marginal rates dropped from 70% (1980) to 37% today
- **Financialization:** The economy shifted from producing things to trading financial instruments
- **Deregulation:** Banking, telecom, media, energy — all deregulated
- **Wage stagnation policies:** Federal minimum wage has been $7.25 since 2009

## 10. International Comparison

### Corruption Perception Index (Transparency International)

The U.S. has been **declining** on Transparency International's Corruption Perception Index:

| Year | U.S. Score (0–100) | U.S. Rank | Notable Comparisons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **2020** | 67 | — | Historic low at the time |
| **2023** | 69 | 24th | Denmark: 90, Finland: 87, New Zealand: 85 |
| **2024** | 65 | — | 4-point drop |
| **2025** | **64** | **29th** | **Lowest since index began in 2012**. Canada: 75, UK: 70, Sweden: 80 |

The global average is 42, so the U.S. isn't the most corrupt country — but it's falling further behind its peers every year.

### What Other Democracies Do Differently

**Campaign Finance:**
- **Canada:** Strict limits on both contributions and spending. Public funding provides the majority of campaign financing. "Third party" spending is capped during election periods.
- **France:** Campaign spending limits, publicly funded elections, ban on TV advertising by candidates.
- **Germany:** Public funding for parties based on vote share, strict transparency requirements.
- **UK:** Strict spending limits per constituency, short campaign periods.
- **U.S.:** After *Citizens United* and *McCutcheon*, effectively unlimited spending by anyone. $15.9 billion spent in 2024 elections.

**Lobbying:**
- Most European countries regulate lobbying more strictly or ban the revolving door entirely
- **Canada:** Requires disclosure of *all* communications intended to influence officials, including grassroots lobbying, with monthly reports detailing meetings and targeted policies
- **EU:** Mandatory Transparency Register for lobbyists
- **U.S.:** Registration and quarterly disclosure, but massive loopholes (grassroots lobbying exempt, "strategic advisors" avoid registration)

**Healthcare:**
- Every other wealthy democracy provides **universal healthcare coverage**
- The U.S. is the only wealthy nation where **medical bankruptcy exists**
- The U.S. is the only wealthy nation that allows **direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical advertising** (only New Zealand also allows it)

**The Revolving Door:**
- **France:** 3-year cooling-off period before officials can join industries they regulated
- **Canada:** 5-year lobbying ban for senior officials
- **U.S.:** 1–2 year cooling-off period, widely circumvented through "strategic advisory" roles

## The System, Summarized

This isn't a collection of individual bad actors. It's a **system** — designed, maintained, and defended by those who profit from it:

1. **Industries spend billions lobbying** Congress to write favorable laws
2. **Congress members trade stocks** based on information they receive in their official capacity
