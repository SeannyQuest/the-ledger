## 1. Wealth Distribution — A Country Owned by the Few

**The top 1% of Americans own 31% of the nation's total wealth** — approximately **$49 trillion**. The top 0.1% alone hold a record **13.8%** of all wealth. That's about 330,000 people controlling more than the bottom 165 million combined.

The **bottom 50%** — over 165 million Americans — collectively own just **2.4%** of the country's wealth. Average wealth for someone in the bottom half: **$60,000**. That's not per year — that's *everything they have*.

The top 20 richest Americans hold a collective **$3 trillion** — more than the GDP of the United Kingdom.

## 2. Income Inequality — The Great Divergence

### CEO vs. Worker Pay

| Year | CEO-to-Worker Pay Ratio |
|------|------------------------|
| 1965 | **21 to 1** |
| 1989 | 61 to 1 |
| 2000 | 366 to 1 |
| 2024 | **281 to 1** |

In 2024, the average CEO of a top-350 firm earned **$23 million** — 79% of which came from stock-based compensation. Since 1978, CEO compensation has surged **1,094%**. Typical worker compensation over that same period? Up **26%**.

A CEO makes more **by lunch on January 2nd** than their average worker makes all year.

### The Minimum Wage Scandal

The federal minimum wage has been **$7.25/hour since 2009** — the longest period without an increase in history. That's **$15,080/year** before taxes, for a full-time worker. The federal poverty line for a family of two is $20,440.

- **Adjusted for inflation** from its 1968 peak: the minimum wage should be **$14.42/hour**
- **Adjusted for productivity**: it should be **$26/hour**
- Its current purchasing power is at a **68-year low**

You read that right. A minimum wage worker today has *less* buying power than one in 1956.

### The Gig Economy Trap

An estimated **54% of gig workers** lack employer-based benefits. Only 40% have health insurance through their gig work. Only 5% have short-term disability coverage. They build the platforms billionaires profit from — without a safety net.

## 3. Healthcare — Pay More, Die Sooner

### Spending vs. Outcomes

The U.S. spends **$14,775 per person per year** on healthcare — more than any country on Earth. The next closest, Switzerland, spends $9,963. The OECD average (excluding the U.S.) is $7,371.

We spend **17.2% of GDP** on healthcare. Germany, the next highest, spends 12.3%.

**What do we get for it?**

- **Life expectancy: 79 years** — the lowest among all peer wealthy nations. Comparable countries average **82.5 years**. Japan: 84.8. Switzerland: 84.1.
- **Administrative costs 5x higher** than the average wealthy country
- **Maternal mortality: 17.9 deaths per 100,000 live births** — the worst in the developed world

### Maternal Mortality: A Racial Crisis

| Group | Deaths per 100,000 Live Births (2024) |
|-------|--------------------------------------|
| Black women | **44.8** |
| Asian women | 18.1 |
| White women | 14.2 |
| Hispanic women | 12.1 |

**Black women are 3.2x more likely to die** from pregnancy-related causes than white women. In the richest country in human history.

### Medical Bankruptcy

- **36% of U.S. households** carry medical debt (2024)
- **31 million adults** borrowed an estimated **$74 billion** in 2024 to cover healthcare costs
- **58% of Americans** worry they'd incur medical debt from a major health event
- Medical bills are the **#1 cause of personal bankruptcy** in America

### Insulin: A Case Study in Greed

Before recent manufacturer caps, a vial of insulin in the U.S. cost **$240** retail for uninsured patients. The same vial in Canada: **$30–$40**. U.S. prices were **6x higher** than Canada's. Even after rebates, U.S. net prices were still **2.3x higher**.

Insulin was discovered in 1921. Its inventors sold the patent for $1 each, hoping it would be accessible to all. A century later, Americans ration it and die.

### The Uninsured

**~27 million Americans** have no health insurance (2024). In every other wealthy nation on Earth, that number is zero.

## 4. Education — Debt as a Birthright

### Student Debt

- **Total outstanding: $1.84 trillion** (Q4 2025)
- **44.6 million borrowers** hold federal student debt
- Average debt per borrower: **$39,547**
- Class of 2024 bachelor's degree graduates: average **$29,560** in debt

### The Cost Explosion

- Average annual cost of a four-year college (inflation-adjusted): **$10,231 in 1980 → $28,775 in 2020** — a **180% increase**
- College tuition has increased **41.7% faster** than the rate of inflation in the 21st century
- 2025–2026 tuition: **$11,950** (public in-state) / **$45,000** (private nonprofit)

In 1980, a student could work a minimum-wage summer job and cover most of their tuition. Today, a minimum-wage worker would need to work **full-time for over 8 months** just to cover in-state tuition — before rent, food, or books.

### School Funding: Your ZIP Code Is Your Destiny

Over **80% of local school funding** comes from property taxes. Wealthy neighborhoods get well-funded schools. Poor neighborhoods get crumbling buildings and teacher shortages.

- **Per-pupil spending can vary by $10,000+** between districts in the same state
- Predominantly white districts receive **billions more** in funding than districts serving students of color, even with comparable student populations

### Teacher Pay

The average public school teacher salary: **$72,030** (2023–24). Adjusted for inflation, teacher pay has **declined** over the past decade. In Mississippi, the average is below **$50,000**. We ask teachers to shape the future and pay them less than an entry-level software developer.

## 5. Housing — Priced Out of the American Dream

### The Numbers

- **Median home price (2024): $412,500** — up **60%** since 2019
- **Average monthly housing payment: $3,270** — requiring an income of **$126,670/year**
- **Median age of first-time homebuyer: 38** — the highest ever recorded
- **Homeownership rate: 65.6%** (Q2 2024), declining, especially for young adults
- Under-35 homeownership rate: **36.3%** — lowest since 2019
- **Housing supply gap: 3.8 million units**

### Rent Burden

- **22.7 million renter households** (over 50% of all renters) are cost-burdened — spending more than 30% of income on rent
- **12.1 million** are severely cost-burdened — spending over **50%** of income on housing
- **83% of households earning under $30,000** are cost-burdened
- 57% of Black renters are cost-burdened vs. 46% of white renters

### Homelessness

**771,480 people** were experiencing homelessness on a single night in January 2024 — an **18% increase** from 2023 and the **highest number ever recorded** (since tracking began in 2007).

- Nearly **150,000 children** were homeless
- **42,150 people over 64** were homeless — nearly half unsheltered
- Chronic homelessness reached its **highest level ever**
- Family homelessness surged **39%** in one year

### Wall Street Is Buying Your Neighborhood

In the first half of 2024, **investors purchased 25% of all homes** — roughly 84,000 per month. Total investor purchases: **1.2 million homes** in 2024, a 9% increase. While institutional investors (100+ homes) represent a smaller slice, the cumulative impact of investor activity — from hedge funds to LLCs — is reshaping entire neighborhoods from owner-occupied communities into rental extraction zones.

## 6. Food Insecurity — Hunger in the World's Richest Country

- **14.1 million children** lived in food-insecure households in 2024
- **18.4% of households with children** experienced food insecurity
- **318,000 households** experienced *very low food security* among children — meaning kids were **hungry, skipped meals, or didn't eat for a whole day**
- 31% of Black households with children were food insecure vs. 14.1% of white households
- 36.8% of single-mother households were food insecure

The U.S. GDP in 2024 was approximately **$29 trillion**. We are the richest nation in the history of human civilization, and 14 million of our children don't have enough to eat.

The USDA has announced it **may stop publishing its annual food security report** — eliminating the data that tracks this crisis.

## 7. Tax Inequality — A System Built for the Rich

### What Billionaires Actually Pay

ProPublica's bombshell investigation revealed: the 25 wealthiest Americans saw their wealth grow by **$401 billion** from 2014–2018. They paid **$13.6 billion** in federal income taxes — a **"true tax rate" of 3.4%** against their wealth growth.

- Jeff Bezos paid **$0 in federal income tax** in 2007 and 2011
- Elon Musk paid **$0** in 2018
- A UC Berkeley study found Forbes 400 members paid an average effective rate of **24%** (2018–2020) — while all other taxpayers paid **30%**

A schoolteacher or firefighter pays a higher effective tax rate than a billionaire.

### Capital Gains vs. Income Tax

The top ordinary income tax rate: **37%**. Long-term capital gains rate: **20%** (plus 3.8% NIIT = 23.8% max). The wealthiest Americans earn most of their money through investments, not salaries — so they pay the *lower* rate.

### The Carried Interest Loophole

Private equity and hedge fund managers get paid in "carried interest" — their cut of profits from managing other people's money. Despite being compensation for services, it's taxed as capital gains (**20%**) instead of ordinary income (**37%**). It also **avoids the 15.3% self-employment tax**. This loophole costs taxpayers billions annually and benefits a tiny class of already-wealthy fund managers. Every president since Obama has promised to close it. None have.

### Corporate Taxes: A Vanishing Act

- U.S. corporate tax revenue as % of GDP: **1.6%** (2021) — vs. OECD average of **3.2%**
- The TCJA slashed the corporate rate from **35% to 21%** in 2017
- Projections: corporate tax revenues will average just **1.25% of GDP** through 2031

In the 1950s, corporate taxes accounted for roughly 30% of federal revenue. Today: **~9%**.

## 8. Labor — The War on Workers

### Union Decline = Wage Stagnation

| Year | Union Membership Rate |
|------|----------------------|
| 1983 | **20.1%** |
| 2024 | **9.9%** |

Private sector union membership: **5.9%** in 2025 — down from 16.5% in 1985.

The correlation is stark: as union membership collapsed, real wages flatlined. The average hourly wage today has roughly the same purchasing power as it did in **1978**. If private-sector union density had stayed at 1979 levels, non-union men would earn an estimated **$2,704 more per year**. For men without a college degree: **$3,172 more**.

Union decline explains roughly **one-third** of the growth in male wage inequality since 1972.

### Corporate Welfare: Amazon & Walmart

Some of the most profitable companies in America pay wages so low that their workers rely on taxpayer-funded safety nets:

- **Walmart:** ~14,500 employees on SNAP benefits; ~29% of Nevada employees on Medicaid
- **Amazon:** 48% of workers used some form of public assistance (2023 survey); 48.4% of Nevada workforce on Medicaid
- **70% of food stamp recipients** working at large retailers were employed **full-time**

The Walton family is worth over $250 billion. Amazon's market cap exceeds $2 trillion. Their workers are on food stamps. The American taxpayer subsidizes their labor costs.

## 9. Social Mobility — The American Dream Is Dead

### The Numbers Don't Lie

Intergenerational earnings elasticity measures how much your parents' income determines yours. Higher = less mobile.

| Country | Earnings Elasticity |
|---------|-------------------|
| Denmark | 0.10–0.20 |
| Finland | 0.17 |
| Norway | 0.16 |
| Canada | 0.20–0.30 |
| Germany | 0.32 |
| **United States** | **0.40–0.60** |

**40–60% of income differences persist across generations in America.** In Denmark, it's 10–20%.

The OECD estimates it takes **5 generations** for a low-income family in the U.S. to reach average income. In Denmark: **2 generations**. In Norway, Finland, Sweden: **3 generations**.

The "Great Gatsby Curve" shows: **the higher a country's inequality, the lower its social mobility.** The U.S. sits at the extreme — high inequality, low mobility. The American Dream is more achievable in Denmark than in America.

### ZIP Code as Destiny

Where you're born in America predicts where you'll end up more than almost any other variable. Children born into the bottom quintile in certain neighborhoods have a **4.4% chance** of reaching the top quintile. In other neighborhoods, that figure exceeds **12%**. Same country. Radically different chances.

## 10. The Oligarchy — Rule by the Rich

### Billionaire Explosion

| Year | U.S. Billionaires | Combined Wealth |
|------|-------------------|----------------|
| 1990 | ~66 | ~$240 billion |
| 2020 | 614 | $2.95 trillion |
| 2025 | **~850–900** | **$6.2+ trillion** |

The number of American billionaires has increased **13x** since 1990. Their combined wealth has grown **25x**.

### Pandemic Profiteering

While millions lost jobs, homes, and lives during COVID-19:

- U.S. billionaire wealth surged **88%** — up **$2.58 trillion** — between March 2020 and March 2024
- The number of billionaires grew from **614 to 737** in the first year alone
- **One-third** of all billionaire wealth gains between 1990 and 2021 occurred during **13 months of the pandemic**

Workers got $1,200 stimulus checks. Billionaires got **$2.58 trillion**.

### The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA)

Sold as a middle-class tax cut. The reality:

- Corporate rate slashed from **35% to 21%** — **permanently**
- Individual tax cuts for the middle class? **Set to expire in 2025**
- 81% of the corporate tax cut benefits went to the **top 10%** of earners
- Top 1% received average cuts of **$61,090** per year
- Middle-income earners ($25K–$50K): average cut of **$359**
- The estate tax exemption was **doubled**, benefiting only the wealthiest estates
- The pass-through deduction sent two-thirds of its benefits to the **richest 10%** of business owners

The TCJA added an estimated **$1.9 trillion** to the national debt over 10 years. When politicians say we "can't afford" universal healthcare or free college, remember where that money went.

## 11. What Could Be Different

### Universal Healthcare

- A single-payer system would save an estimated **$450 billion per year** (Yale)
- Over 10 years: **~$5 trillion** in savings
- An estimated **68,000+ lives saved annually**
- In 2020 alone, **212,000 Americans could have been saved** if we had universal healthcare
- **Every other wealthy nation** provides it. We spend more and get less.

**Cost to provide universal healthcare: less than what we currently spend.** It's not a cost problem. It's a political will problem.

### Free Public College

- Last-dollar tuition-free program: **~$28 billion/year** in the first year
- First-dollar tuition-free: **~$58 billion/year**
- Full debt-free program: **~$75 billion/year**
- The College For All Act: **$700 billion** total

For context: the 2017 TCJA costs the Treasury roughly **$190 billion per year** in lost revenue. We could make public college tuition-free **twice over** for what we gave away in corporate tax cuts.

### A Living Wage

If the minimum wage tracked productivity, it would be **$26/hour** — that's $54,080/year. Studies show a higher minimum wage:

- Reduces poverty and food insecurity
- Decreases reliance on public assistance (i.e., corporate welfare)
- Stimulates local economies (low-wage workers spend most of what they earn)
- Has minimal impact on employment when phased in gradually

It is not that we cannot afford these things. We are the richest country in the history of the world. **We choose not to provide them.**
